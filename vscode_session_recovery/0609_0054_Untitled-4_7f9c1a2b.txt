# Section Writing Guide: Rules from Zion & CARE
_Derived from forensic analysis of two SNL-lab accepted papers: Zion (MICRO) and CARE (TPDS). This is the authoritative reference for section-level writing conventions. Use alongside voice_profile.md and editorial_principles.md._

## How to Use This Guide

This guide encodes the section-by-section writing rules observed in two accepted systems papers: Zion (MICRO) and CARE (TPDS), both from the SNL lab at UCSB. Each rule is backed by a direct quote from the paper so you can see exactly what published prose looks like when the rule is followed. Use the Quick-Reference Templates when starting a new section draft — they are fill-in-the-blank skeletons that enforce correct structure before you write a word of real prose. Use the Common Failure Modes sections for self-review: read each failure mode, then search your own draft for the same symptom.

## Chapter 0: Tense Rules — When to Use Present vs. Past Tense

*One of the most consistent error types in PhD student drafts is incorrect tense. This chapter documents the exact tense conventions used in Zion and CARE, section by section. Every rule below is verified against direct quotation from the published texts. When in doubt, return to this chapter before editing a draft.*

---

### Why Tense Errors Are High-Stakes

Tense errors are not cosmetic. A past-tense sentence in a Design section reads as a lab notebook entry ("we did X"), not a system description ("X does Y"). A present-tense sentence in an Experimental Procedure paragraph creates the false impression that the simulation is ongoing rather than completed. Reviewers notice these errors because they create a mismatch between what the grammar promises and what the paper actually claims.

The rules below reduce to three master principles:

1. **System behavior and established facts: always present tense.** A hardware mechanism does not stop functioning because the paper was written in the past.
2. **Your experimental procedure: present tense.** Both Zion and CARE describe their methodology in present tense ("We evaluate X using Y", "We warm up each core using 50M instructions"). Treat methodology as a reusable protocol description, not a historical narrative.
3. **Results reported from completed experiments: present tense.** The data are a permanent fact of record. "Figure 5 shows..." not "Figure 5 showed..."; "Zion achieves 43.2%..." not "Zion achieved 43.2%..."

---

### Section 1: Abstract

---

**RULE T-A1: The field-level opening sentence uses present tense.**

The opening sentence describes a permanent condition of the field — a tension, a trend, or a lasting challenge. These facts do not change. Both papers open in present tense.

**EVIDENCE (Zion):** "As the gap between processor and memory performance **widens**, optimizing data access performance **becomes** increasingly critical."

**EVIDENCE (CARE):** "Improving cache performance **is** a lasting research topic."

**APPLY:** Never open the abstract in past tense. "Memory access latency *was* a longstanding problem" sounds as if the problem has been solved. The opening sentence describes the world as it is now and always has been.

---

**RULE T-A2: The prior-technique description in the abstract uses present tense.**

Prior techniques continue to exist and continue to behave in the ways described. Describing them in past tense implies they are obsolete or withdrawn.

**EVIDENCE (Zion):** "Hardware prefetching **is** a widely used technique to hide long-latency off-chip memory accesses, but state-of-the-art prefetchers **struggle** with diverse and dynamic access patterns."

**EVIDENCE (CARE):** "While utilizing data locality to enhance cache performance **becomes** more and more difficult, data access concurrency **provides** a new opportunity..."

**APPLY:** Use "is," "are," "struggles," "lacks," "fails" — not "was," "were," "struggled," "lacked," "failed." The failure mode you describe is still active in the field.

---

**RULE T-A3: Your system description in the abstract uses present tense.**

Your system exists. It behaves in described ways now and on every future run. Use present tense for all mechanism and component descriptions.

**EVIDENCE (Zion):** "At its core, Zion **uses** Independent Temporal-Spatial Modules (ITSM) for broad pattern coverage... Moreover, Zion **leverages** runtime feedback to dynamically **guide** prefetching decisions..."

**EVIDENCE (CARE):** "CARE **learns** the re-reference behavior and PMC value of each miss access to guide future replacement decisions. CARE **augments** existing cache insertion and hit-promotion policies..."

**APPLY:** "Zion uses..." not "Zion used...". "CARE learns..." not "CARE learned...". The system is not a historical artifact.

---

**RULE T-A4: Evaluation scope and results in the abstract use present tense for the framing sentence, and present tense for specific numbers.**

The framing sentence introduces the evaluation as a standing fact. The specific numbers are permanent experimental results. Both use present tense.

**EVIDENCE (Zion):** "Extensive multi-core evaluations **show** that Zion consistently **outperforms** state-of-the-art prefetchers, **achieving** up to 43.2% performance improvement on SPEC and 43.0% on self-attention workloads..."

**EVIDENCE (CARE):** "In a 4-core system, CARE **improves** IPC by 10.3% over LRU replacement. In 8 and 16-core systems... CARE **outperforms** LRU by 13.0% and 17.1%, respectively."

**APPLY:** "Zion achieves 43.2%" — not "Zion achieved 43.2%." The result is a permanent fact of the published record. Past tense would imply the result might change.

---

### Section 2: Introduction

---

**RULE T-I1: Problem statements in the introduction use present tense.**

The problem motivating your paper is still a problem. Write it in present tense.

**EVIDENCE (Zion):** "Modern memory-intensive workloads further **exacerbate** this problem, placing memory access latency at the forefront of performance bottlenecks."

**EVIDENCE (Zion):** "executing attention-based computations on CPUs **poses** significant challenges due to the quadratic memory complexity inherent in self-attention mechanisms..."

**EVIDENCE (CARE):** "the shared LLC **can** observe very mixed access patterns. The mixed access patterns **can downgrade** the effectiveness of locality-based schemes..."

**APPLY:** Every sentence that describes why the problem matters uses present tense. The world still has memory wall problems. Self-attention still has quadratic complexity.

---

**RULE T-I2: Prior-work descriptions in the introduction use present tense.**

Hardware prefetchers still exist. Cache management schemes still make replacement decisions. Their mechanisms and limitations remain present-tense facts.

**EVIDENCE (Zion):** "Hardware prefetching **is** a widely used technique to mitigate memory access latency... Despite numerous proposed hardware prefetchers, we **observe** three critical limitations..."

**EVIDENCE (Zion):** "most prefetchers **lack** the ability to utilize runtime feedback, leading to limited adaptability..."

**EVIDENCE (CARE):** "Significant research **focuses** on taking advantage of data locality, resulting in many schemes that **detect** memory access patterns..."

**APPLY:** "Prior prefetchers lack adaptability" — present tense. Not "prior prefetchers lacked adaptability." The limitation is structural, not historical.

---

**RULE T-I3: Your own contribution claims in the introduction use present tense.**

Contribution bullets describe what your paper does and what your system delivers — standing facts, not past events.

**EVIDENCE (Zion):**
- "We **propose** ITSM, a novel design based on independent temporal-spatial modules, to enhance prediction accuracy..."
- "We **propose** a runtime adaptive mechanism that **dynamically adjusts** prefetch and insertion decisions..."
- "We **extensively evaluate** and **demonstrate** that Zion **consistently outperforms** SOTA prefetchers..."

**EVIDENCE (CARE):**
- "We **introduce** the pure miss contribution (PMC), a novel and accurate metric..."
- "We **present** CARE, a comprehensive cache management framework..."
- "Our evaluations **show** that CARE **substantially improves** upon existing state-of-the-art..."

**APPLY:** Contribution bullets are written in simple present ("We propose", "We introduce", "We show"). Never in past tense ("We proposed", "We introduced", "We showed"). The paper makes these claims as of the moment of reading, not as of the moment of writing.

---

**RULE T-I4: Evaluation summary sentences in the introduction use present tense.**

**EVIDENCE (Zion):** "The results **demonstrate** that Zion, with its comprehensive and adaptive design, **consistently outperforms** state-of-the-art (SOTA) hardware prefetchers... Notably, in a 4-core configuration, Zion **achieves** performance improvements of 18.2%, 10.1%, 8.0% and 5.1% over these SOTAs, respectively."

**APPLY:** Numbers in the introduction's evaluation paragraph are stated in present tense ("achieves"), not past tense ("achieved"). This is consistent with the abstract rule: results are permanent facts.

---

### Section 3: Background and Related Work

---

**RULE T-B1: Defining established concepts uses present tense.**

Concepts such as MLP, C-AMAT, cache replacement policies, and memory access patterns are described as they currently work, not as they worked when first published.

**EVIDENCE (CARE):** "Memory Level Parallelism (MLP) **can be used** to measure miss concurrency. MLP **captures** the number of outstanding cache misses that **can be** generated and executed in an overlapped manner. Some misses **are** isolated, while some **occur** concurrently with other misses."

**EVIDENCE (CARE):** "Active pure miss cycles **are** the cycles that only contain the pure miss cycles... these cycles **cause** more performance degradation. On the other hand, the active non-pure miss cycles **do not** introduce heavy degradation..."

**EVIDENCE (Zion):** "Hardware prefetching **is** a widely adopted technique for mitigating memory access latency by proactively fetching data before it is requested, with state-of-the-art prefetchers **employing** diverse and distinct design strategies."

**APPLY:** Background subsections use present tense throughout for definitional content. The concepts being explained are permanent properties of the systems described.

---

**RULE T-B2: Describing prior systems uses present tense for mechanism, architecture, and limitation.**

Prior systems continue to function as described. Their architecture does not change retroactively.

**EVIDENCE (Zion):**
- "IPCP [7] **classifies** instruction pointers into categories: constant stride, complex stride, and global stream, **enabling** tailored prefetching strategies for each pattern type."
- "Bingo [9] **enhances** spatial pattern learning by **associating** short-range (IP-based) and long-range access correlations."
- "SPP [6] **predicts** irregular L2 strides by **encoding** access patterns into signatures..."
- "Berti [8] **tracks** local deltas to generate timely and precise prefetches."

**EVIDENCE (CARE):**
- "Hawkeye **simulates** and **learns** Belady's optimal solution for a long history of memory accesses..."
- "SRRIP [19] **statically predicts** an 'intermediate' re-reference interval at cache insertion time and **updates** the re-reference prediction on subsequent accesses."
- "Mockingjay [41] **mimics** Belady's optimal solution effectively and **introduces** a cache replacement policy based on multi-class re-reference prediction."

**APPLY:** Mechanism verbs for prior systems: "classifies," "enhances," "predicts," "tracks," "learns," "simulates," "mimics." Not "classified," "enhanced," "predicted." The system still does these things when run.

---

**RULE T-B3: Critiquing prior work uses present tense for structural limitations.**

A structural limitation of a prior system is permanent. It cannot be fixed without abandoning the design.

**EVIDENCE (Zion):**
- "IPCP **employs** a unified IP table... which **can cause** early evictions of infrequent but potentially valuable entries due to conflicts."
- "its count-based confidence mechanism **inherently underestimates** entries." [SPP]
- "its count-based confidence mechanism **ignores** rare but accurate stream patterns in favor of frequent deltas reducing overall effectiveness." [Berti]
- "traditional unified-table designs often **prematurely evict** infrequent yet important entries, particularly if a count-based confidence mechanism is employed."

**EVIDENCE (CARE):**
- "MLP **does not consider** hit-miss overlapping. Therefore, the MLP-aware replacement policy **can be** further improved."
- "the cost estimation model of LACS **is not** cycle-accurate, it **is** impossible to estimate the penalty of the misses on performance accurately."

**APPLY:** "The mechanism lacks X" not "The mechanism lacked X." The flaw is structural, not historical.

---

**RULE T-B4: Gap-forward pointer sentences at subsection close use present tense.**

**EVIDENCE (CARE):** "MLP-based cost **can** identify costly misses by considering the miss-miss overlapping. However, we **find** that hit-miss overlapping **impacts** the cost of misses as well... Therefore, we **need** a holistic metric that **is** able to catch all types of overlapping..."

**EVIDENCE (CARE):** "In this work, we **present** pure miss contribution metric (details in Section IV), which **is** inspired by C-AMAT model, and it **shows** the great potential to be incorporated with cache optimization frameworks."

**APPLY:** Gap statements use "we find" (present), "we need" (present), "we present" (present) — not "we found," "we needed," "we presented."

---

### Section 4: Design

---

**RULE T-D1: System mechanism descriptions use present tense throughout.**

The design section describes how the system works. It uses present tense exclusively for mechanism, component, and operational descriptions.

**EVIDENCE (Zion):**
- "We **propose** Zion, a comprehensive, adaptive and lightweight prefetcher **designed** to accelerate memory-intensive workloads."
- "Independent Temporal-Spatial Modules (ITSM) **consist** of multiple prediction modules that **leverage** IP to predict memory access patterns by tracking both deltas and strides."
- "ITSM **distributes** the prediction process across these five distinct MTs, each **predicting** the most likely next delta or stride for a given IP..."
- "Zion **continuously monitors** inactive MTs, and if a new, in-progress, period monitoring **reaches** the required threshold, MT **is reactivated**."

**EVIDENCE (CARE):**
- "CARE **learns** the re-reference behavior and PMC value of each miss access to guide future replacement decisions."
- "CARE **augments** existing cache insertion and hit-promotion policies to reserve a small subset of performance-critical blocks..."
- "For every new cache access, CARE first **extracts** the signature from the PC of the cache request. The signature... **is used** to index into the SHT."
- "CARE **assigns**/**updates** the EPV counter for each block based on the prediction information from SBP."

**APPLY:** Every sentence describing what the system does at runtime uses present tense. This is the most important tense rule in the paper. Violations are immediately visible to reviewers because they make the design read like a lab notebook.

---

**RULE T-D2: Condition-action hardware descriptions use present tense for both the condition and the action.**

**EVIDENCE (Zion):** "If a matching entry **is** found in the MT, the confidence score of the corresponding entry **is incremented**. Otherwise, a new entry **is inserted** into the corresponding MT."

**EVIDENCE (Zion):** "When 25% or 12.5% of MSHR **is** available, an MT **must have** the second or third level, respectively, to remain active."

**EVIDENCE (CARE):** "If the PMC value of a miss **is** smaller than PMC_low, the PMCS of the corresponding cache block **is** 0. If the PMC value of a miss **is** larger than PMC_high, the PMCS of the corresponding cache block **is set** to 3."

**EVIDENCE (CARE):** "When a miss access **allocates** an MSHR entry, the PMC counter associated with the miss **will be initialized** to zero."

**APPLY:** Both the condition clause and the action clause use present tense. "If X is found, Y is incremented" — not "If X was found, Y was incremented."

---

**RULE T-D3: Hardware state changes described as permanent properties use present tense.**

**EVIDENCE (CARE):** "A zero RC value **indicates** the future blocks associated with this signature are rarely reused. A positive RC counter **implies** that the future blocks... have data locality..."

**EVIDENCE (CARE):** "An EPV of zero **implies** that a cache block **has** the lowest eviction priority. An EPV of saturation **implies** that a cache block **has** a high eviction priority and **will be** evicted sooner."

**APPLY:** Counter semantics, field meanings, and priority assignments are permanent properties of the design. Always present tense.

---

### Section 5: Methodology

---

**RULE T-M1: Simulation setup description uses past tense for what you did and present tense for the simulator's capabilities.**

The experimental setup was established in the past. The simulator exists and operates in the present.

**EVIDENCE (Zion):** "We **evaluate** the performance of Zion using the ChampSim simulator [26], which **has been employed** in the 2nd and 3rd Data Prefetching Championships."

*Note:* Zion uses simple present ("We evaluate") for the setup framing, treating the evaluation as a standing methodological choice rather than a completed historical act. This is the published form.

**EVIDENCE (CARE):** "We **evaluate** CARE against prior cache management schemes using the version of ChampSim [4] used for the 1st instruction prefetching competition."

*Note:* CARE likewise uses present tense ("We evaluate") for the setup framing sentence. Both papers treat methodology as a permanent record, not a historical narrative.

**APPLY:** Use present tense for the framing sentence of methodology ("We evaluate X using Y"). The present-tense framing establishes the methodology as a standing description of what the paper does, not what the authors did one time in the past.

---

**RULE T-M2: Warm-up and measurement procedure descriptions mix present and past in a specific pattern.**

Both papers use simple present for the procedure-description sentences in methodology.

**EVIDENCE (Zion):** "For every benchmark cache **is** initialized through warm-up of 50M sim-point instructions and statistics **are** kept for the next 200M sim-point instructions."

**EVIDENCE (ZION):** "For all simulations, the first 20% of instructions **are** used for warm-up, and the remaining 80% **are** used for evaluation."

**EVIDENCE (CARE):** "we **warm up** each core using 50M instructions from each workload, and then **run** simulation over the next 200M instructions."

**APPLY:** Procedure sentences in the methodology section use present tense in both papers. "We warm up" not "We warmed up." "Statistics are kept" not "Statistics were kept." Treat the methodology as a reusable protocol, not a past experiment.

---

**RULE T-M3: Workload selection and configuration descriptions use present tense.**

**EVIDENCE (Zion):** "We **select** 37 traces, each **exhibiting** LLC miss per kilo instructions (MPKI) greater than 1 in the baseline system without prefetching."

**EVIDENCE (Zion):** "We **compare** Zion against four SOTA hardware prefetchers: IPCP [7], Bingo [9], SPP [6], and Berti [8]. For comparison, we **employ** a simple next-line prefetcher at the L1D cache..."

**EVIDENCE (CARE):** "We **evaluate** CARE using a diverse set of memory-intensive workloads spanning SPEC CPU2006 [43], SPEC CPU2017 [6], and GAP [9] benchmark suites, which **have** at least 1 LLC miss per kilo instructions..."

**EVIDENCE (CARE):** "We **select** LRU as the baseline for performance comparison. We **compare** CARE against three state-of-the-art LLC management schemes..."

**APPLY:** All methodology setup sentences — simulator, workloads, baselines, warm-up — use present tense in both papers. This is the dominant pattern; do not deviate.

---

### Section 6: Evaluation

---

**RULE T-E1: Figure-pointer sentences ALWAYS use present tense regardless of section.**

This is the single most frequently violated rule in student drafts. Figure references are always present tense. A figure is a permanent object in the paper; it "shows" or "presents" or "illustrates" things right now, not in the past.

**EVIDENCE (Zion):**
- "**Figure 5(a) presents** the normalized speedup of all evaluated prefetchers running 37 homogeneous SPEC workloads under various system configurations..."
- "**Figure 5(b) illustrates** the geometric mean speedup across various prefetchers on homogeneous self-attention workloads."
- "**Figure 6 shows** that Zion achieves LLC coverage rates of 73.6%, 84.0%, and 90.5% on SPEC 2006, SPEC 2017, and self-attention workloads, respectively."
- "**Figure 7 shows** the weighted speedup over the no-prefetch baseline..."

**EVIDENCE (CARE):**
- "**Figure 7 shows** the normalized IPC of schemes followed by the geometric mean across all the SPEC workloads."
- "**Figure 8 compares** the LLC pure miss rate (pMR) with different schemes..."
- "**Figure 9 shows** the detailed results on the multi-copy GAP workloads."
- "**Figure 10 shows** the normalized weighted IPC comparison between different schemes..."
- "**Figure 1 shows** several concurrent cache accesses."
- "**Figure 3 illustrates** the percentage of LLC misses that have hit-miss overlapping..."

**APPLY:** Search your draft for "Figure N showed," "Figure N demonstrated," "Figure N presented." Replace every instance with "shows," "demonstrates," "presents." No exceptions. This rule applies in every section of the paper.

---

**RULE T-E2: Reporting performance numbers uses present tense.**

Numbers reported from evaluation results are present-tense facts of the published record.

**EVIDENCE (Zion):**
- "Across all configurations, Zion consistently **outperforms** all SOTA designs, **achieves** speedup of 25.5%, 38.5%, 40.9%, 43.2% and 32.5% over the baseline, respectively."
- "In particular, under the optimal 4-core configuration, Zion **surpasses** IPCP, Bingo, SPP, and Berti by 18.2%, 10.1%, 8.0%, and 5.1%, respectively."
- "Zion **achieves** an average speedup of 63.8% over the baseline, **outperforming** IPCP, Bingo, SPP, and Berti by 22.3%, 15.0%, 9.9%, and 5.5%, respectively."

**EVIDENCE (CARE):**
- "CARE **achieves** a geometric mean speedup of 10.3% over the LRU on the 30 memory-intensive benchmarks..."
- "In the 4-core configuration, CARE **achieves** an improvement over LRU by 10.3% on average. As the number of cores increases, the gains **are** 13.0%, and 17.1%, respectively."
- "On a 4-core system with prefetching, CARE **improves** performance over LRU by 8.7%..."

**APPLY:** "Zion achieves 43.2%" — present tense. Never "Zion achieved 43.2%." The result is a permanent property of the system as evaluated.

---

**RULE T-E3: Explaining trends and making comparative observations uses present tense.**

**EVIDENCE (Zion):**
- "As the core count **increases**, pattern interference **increases** and prefetcher adaptability **becomes** increasingly important."
- "the memory subsystem saturation, at a higher core count **degrades** the performance at 8-core for all prefetchers."
- "the 6.4× smaller L2 cache capacity **limits** the gains of SPP, Berti, and Zion, resulting in a small performance gap."

**EVIDENCE (CARE):**
- "Figure 7 **demonstrates** that since CARE **considers** both locality and concurrency in cache management, CARE **outperforms** state-of-the-art schemes."
- "The irregular access patterns of graph-analytic applications **challenge** the re-reference prediction."
- "The overall performance of the cache management scheme **correlates** well with the change in LLC pure misses and the average PMC value..."

**APPLY:** Trend explanations and causal reasoning in evaluation use present tense. "As cores increase, interference increases" — not "as cores increased, interference increased."

---

**RULE T-E4: Causal explanations in evaluation use present tense.**

**EVIDENCE (Zion):** "These results **demonstrate** that LLMs with equivalent internal architectures **achieve** consistent speedups in self-attention mechanisms."

**EVIDENCE (CARE):** "CARE **performs** better than state-of-the-art schemes, which **can be** attributed to the following two factors. First is its concurrency awareness, which **minimizes** the performance loss caused by misses. Second is the conservative nature of the hit policy."

**EVIDENCE (CARE):** "M-CARE **considers** the miss concurrency but **ignores** the hit-miss overlapping. Therefore, M-CARE **makes** the replacement decisions at a coarse granularity. The accurate analysis of data concurrency by PMC **gives** the performance of CARE an advantage..."

**APPLY:** Causal explanations — "because X, Y happens" — always in present tense. Reviewers read causal claims closely. Past-tense causal sentences ("because X happened, Y occurred") imply the causal relationship was situational rather than structural.

---

### Section 7: Conclusion

---

**RULE T-C1: Restating contributions in the conclusion uses present tense.**

**EVIDENCE (Zion):** "This paper **presents** a Zion prefetcher, a comprehensive and adaptive hardware prefetcher. By **integrating** ITSM with five specialized prediction modules, Zion **achieves** comprehensive coverage across diverse memory access patterns. Additionally, Zion **dynamically tunes** prefetching behavior based on runtime feedback to mitigate memory pressure and **enhance** performance, while remaining lightweight."

**EVIDENCE (CARE):** "In this paper, we **emphasize** the importance of data concurrency to memory performance. We **propose** Pure Miss Contribution (PMC), a comprehensive metric used to weigh the performance cost of each cache miss. We first **develop** a detailed measurement mechanism for PMC. Then, we **utilize** PMC to build CARE, a locality and concurrency-aware, lightweight cache management framework."

**APPLY:** "This paper presents..." not "This paper presented...". "We propose..." not "We proposed...". The contribution exists in the present tense of the published paper.

---

**RULE T-C2: Evaluation summary statements in the conclusion use present tense.**

**EVIDENCE (Zion):** "Evaluations **show** that Zion **consistently outperforms** state-of-the-art prefetchers across both SPEC and self-attention workloads."

**EVIDENCE (CARE):** "It **outperforms** state-of-the-art cache management schemes. Experimental and analysis results **show** CARE **has** a true potential for data-intensive scalable computing systems."

**APPLY:** The conclusion does not report results in past tense. "Evaluations show..." not "Evaluations showed...". The evidence remains in the present.

---

**RULE T-C3: Forward-looking statements in the conclusion use present tense for current capability and future tense for forward projection.**

**EVIDENCE (Zion):** "Overall, Zion **provides** a practical and extensible foundation for future CPU-based systems targeting efficient memory-intensive computing and on-device LLM inference." — current capability stated in present ("provides"); the forward projection is embedded in the noun phrase ("for future systems").

**EVIDENCE (CARE):** "CARE **has** a true potential for data-intensive scalable computing systems." — present tense for current properties.

**APPLY:** "Our system provides a foundation for..." (present tense for current state). If projecting explicitly: "Future work will explore..." (future tense). Never "Our system provided a foundation for..."

### Common Tense Failure Modes

---

**TENSE FAILURE 1: "Figure N showed that..."**

*Diagnosis:* The student treated the figure as a historical artifact from a past experiment rather than a permanent object in the paper.

*Symptom in review:* Reads as a lab report, not a paper.

*Fix:* "Figure N shows that..." The figure is present. It shows things now. This applies in every section — methodology, design, evaluation, conclusion.

---

**TENSE FAILURE 2: "We proposed / We introduced / We demonstrated..."**

*Diagnosis:* The student wrote the contribution section as if describing past events. Common in conclusions and abstracts when the student is summarizing the paper as a finished object.

*Symptom in review:* Reads as if the contribution is already obsolete or superseded.

*Fix:* "We propose / We introduce / We demonstrate..." Contribution claims are always present tense in systems papers, even when restating them in the conclusion.

---

**TENSE FAILURE 3: Past tense for prior system mechanisms**

*Diagnosis:* "IPCP classified instruction pointers..." or "Bingo utilized a unified hardware table..." — as if the prior system has been decommissioned.

*Symptom in review:* Implies the prior system no longer functions, which is factually wrong and signals unfamiliarity with the field.

*Fix:* "IPCP classifies..." / "Bingo utilizes..." Prior systems continue to exist and function. Describe them in present tense.

---

**TENSE FAILURE 4: Past tense for evaluation results**

*Diagnosis:* "In the 4-core configuration, Zion achieved 43.2% speedup..." — the student treated the experiment as a one-time past event.

*Symptom in review:* Implies the result might be different on a re-run, or that the system no longer achieves this. Undermines confidence in reproducibility.

*Fix:* "Zion achieves 43.2% speedup..." The result is a permanent published fact. Present tense.

---

**TENSE FAILURE 5: Past tense for design mechanism descriptions**

*Diagnosis:* "ITSM distributed the prediction process across five MTs..." — the student narrated the design as a historical story.

*Symptom in review:* Reads as a post-hoc description of something that was built and then abandoned. Weakens the authority of the design section.

*Fix:* "ITSM distributes the prediction process across five MTs..." The mechanism is a permanent property of the system.

## Chapter 1: Abstract & Introduction

*A writing style guide for systems and computer architecture PhD students, derived from forensic sentence-level analysis of two accepted papers: Zion (MICRO) and CARE (TPDS). Every rule in this chapter is grounded in direct evidence from those texts. Open this document when drafting or revising; do not rely on memory.*

---

### Abstract: Structure and Rules

The abstract is an eight-sentence compression of the entire paper. Each sentence has a single assigned job. Violating the sequence — starting with your proposal, burying the numbers, hedging the outcome — produces abstracts that reviewers describe as "lacks clear contribution" or "unclear novelty." Both Zion and CARE obey the same eight-slot structure. The slots are fixed; the words inside them vary by paper.

---

**RULE A1: Open with one sentence about why the domain matters. Never open with "In this paper..."**

The first sentence establishes the reader's stakes before introducing any technique or system. Two equally valid framings exist: *trend framing* (something is getting worse) and *permanence framing* (this problem has always mattered). Both anchor the reader before any claim is made.

**EVIDENCE (Zion):** "As the gap between processor and memory performance widens, optimizing data access performance becomes increasingly critical."

**EVIDENCE (CARE):** "Improving cache performance is a lasting research topic."

**APPLY:** Choose trend framing when a recent development (new workloads, new hardware generation) sharpens the problem. Choose permanence framing when the problem is fundamental and the novelty lies in the angle of attack.

---

**RULE A2: Sentence 2 names the existing technique and its limitation in a single breath.**

Do not spend two sentences arriving at the limitation. The reader already knows the field exists; your job is to locate the fault line. Both papers follow the same compound structure: `[Technique] is [characterization], but [state-of-the-art] [failure verb] with [specific failure mode].`

**EVIDENCE (Zion):** "Hardware prefetching is a widely used technique to hide long-latency off-chip memory accesses, but state-of-the-art prefetchers struggle with diverse and dynamic access patterns."

**EVIDENCE (CARE):** "While utilizing data locality to enhance cache performance becomes more and more difficult, data access concurrency provides a new opportunity for cache performance optimization."

**APPLY:** The CARE variant is structurally equivalent but inverts the polarity: instead of naming what the technique fails to do, it names what the technique has not yet exploited. Use the CARE variant when your contribution is an *additive* insight rather than a *replacement*.

---

**RULE A3: Translate the limitation into a concrete cost immediately — one sentence, no delay.**

After naming the limitation, the next sentence converts it from a description into a consequence. This sentence creates the motivation. Without it, the abstract describes a situation; with it, the abstract argues for action.

**EVIDENCE (Zion):** "Their limited adaptability leads to excessive storage overhead and reduced effectiveness under memory-intensive workloads."

**EVIDENCE (CARE):** *(The cost is embedded in the framing: "becomes more and more difficult" — the difficulty itself is the cost. CARE moves directly to the opportunity sentence.)*

**APPLY:** If you cannot write this sentence in one clause, your limitation is not specific enough. The pattern is: `Their [limitation noun] leads to [concrete overhead or degradation noun].`

---

**RULE A4: The proposal sentence names the system and uses exactly three adjectives.**

The three-adjective pattern is not stylistic decoration. Each adjective maps to one of the three limitations that will be enumerated in the introduction. The reader unconsciously registers this parallelism. Breaking the pattern to four adjectives or dropping to two destroys the architectural promise.

**EVIDENCE (Zion):** "We propose Zion, a comprehensive, adaptive, and lightweight hardware prefetcher for memory-intensive workloads."

**EVIDENCE (CARE):** "In this work, we propose a novel concurrency-aware cache management framework that outperforms state-of-the-art locality-only cache management schemes."

**APPLY:** Zion's three adjectives — comprehensive, adaptive, lightweight — map to: (1) pattern coverage, (2) runtime adaptability, (3) storage overhead. CARE uses a single compound adjective plus a relative clause that carries the performance claim. Either pattern is acceptable. Zion's pattern is preferred when each adjective maps cleanly to a distinct design pillar.

---

**RULE A5: Mechanisms are two sentences. Use "At its core" for the primary mechanism; use "Moreover" for the secondary mechanism. Alternatively, enumerate with First/Next/Then.**

The mechanism sentences are the technical core of the abstract. They exist to distinguish your approach from prior work. They should name the key artifact (a module, a metric, a mechanism) by its actual name, not a paraphrase.

**EVIDENCE (Zion):** "At its core, Zion uses Independent Temporal-Spatial Modules (ITSM) for broad pattern coverage and runtime adaptability to diverse memory access patterns. Moreover, Zion leverages runtime feedback to dynamically guide prefetching decisions and maintain efficiency under memory pressure."

**EVIDENCE (CARE):** "First, we investigate the merit of data access concurrency and pinpoint that reducing the miss rate may not necessarily lead to better overall performance. Next, we introduce the pure miss contribution (PMC) metric, a lightweight and versatile concurrency-aware indicator, to accurately measure the cost of each outstanding miss access by considering data concurrency. Then, we present CARE, a dynamic adjustable, concurrency-aware, low-overhead cache management framework with the help of the PMC metric."

**APPLY:** Use the Zion pattern (At its core / Moreover) when your contributions cluster into one primary design and one secondary design. Use the CARE pattern (First / Next / Then) when contributions unfold sequentially — an insight leads to a metric, which leads to a framework.

---

**RULE A6: Include one evaluation scope sentence before the numbers.**

Do not begin evaluation with bare numbers. Name the benchmark suites, the dimensions of comparison, and the direction of the result first. This sentence contextualizes the numbers that follow and prevents the reader from encountering raw data without a frame.

**EVIDENCE (Zion):** "Extensive multi-core evaluations show that Zion consistently outperforms state-of-the-art prefetchers, achieving up to 43.2% performance improvement on SPEC and 43.0% on self-attention workloads, while maintaining low overhead and broad effectiveness."

**EVIDENCE (CARE):** "We evaluate CARE with extensive experiments across different application domains and show significant performance gains with the consideration of data concurrency."

**APPLY:** The word "extensively" or "extensive" is required. "Consistently outperforms" is required. The benchmarks must be named. Do not write "we evaluate our approach on several benchmarks."

---

**RULE A7: End with specific numbers across 2–3 configurations, using "respectively."**

The final sentence must contain at least two concrete numbers from distinct experimental configurations. The word "respectively" is mandatory when presenting parallel results; it signals to reviewers that you ran multiple conditions and are reporting them honestly.

**EVIDENCE (Zion):** "...achieving up to 43.2% performance improvement on SPEC and 43.0% on self-attention workloads, while maintaining low overhead and broad effectiveness."

**EVIDENCE (CARE):** "In a 4-core system, CARE improves IPC by 10.3% over LRU replacement. In 8 and 16-core systems where more concurrent data accesses exist, CARE outperforms LRU by 13.0% and 17.1%, respectively."

**APPLY:** CARE's pattern is superior for scalability results because it shows a trend (10.3% → 13.0% → 17.1%) embedded in the structure. When your system improves with scale, use CARE's multi-configuration format. When your system shows peak gains, use Zion's "up to X%" format.

---

**RULE A8: Zero hedging in the abstract. No "may," "could," "might," "potentially," "we believe."**

Every claim in the abstract must be stated as fact. Hedging signals that you are not confident in your own results. Reviewers interpret hedged abstracts as evidence that the evaluation is weak.

**EVIDENCE (Zion):** All claims are stated flatly: "struggle," "leads to," "consistently outperforms," "achieving up to."

**EVIDENCE (CARE):** All claims are stated flatly: "outperforms," "improves IPC by," "CARE outperforms LRU by."

**APPLY:** If you catch yourself writing "may improve" or "can potentially reduce," the fix is to replace the verb with the result: "improves by X%" or "reduces by X%." If you do not yet have the number, leave the slot blank until you do. Do not fill it with hedging.

---

### Introduction: Paragraph-by-Paragraph Blueprint

The introduction follows a six-paragraph architecture. Each paragraph has a mandatory function. The paragraphs must appear in order. Do not merge them; do not reorder them. The introduction ends on the final contribution bullet — there is no roadmap paragraph.

---

**PARAGRAPH 1 — Problem Escalation**

**RULE I1: Name the canonical problem first; then introduce a new workload or context that makes it worse; then name the technical reason it is harder.**

The opening paragraph is not an opportunity to survey the field. It is a three-beat escalation: (1) the known problem, (2) a new pressure that sharpens it, (3) the technical mechanism behind that sharpening. Each beat adds specificity.

**EVIDENCE (Zion):** "The unbalanced technological advancements between processor speed and memory performance over the past decades have led to the well-known Memory Wall problem [1]. Modern memory-intensive workloads further exacerbate this problem, placing memory access latency at the forefront of performance bottlenecks [2]. Recently, the emergence of Large Language Models (LLMs) has introduced attention-based workloads, representing an important new category of memory-intensive workloads. [...] Nevertheless, executing attention-based computations on CPUs poses significant challenges due to the quadratic memory complexity inherent in self-attention mechanisms [5], which greatly intensifies data movement between on-chip cache hierarchies and off-chip DRAM."

**EVIDENCE (CARE):** "Intensive research has been conducted to address the memory wall problem [50], of which improving locality and concurrency are two fundamental approaches. Cache hierarchies utilize data locality to minimize the long delay of off-chip main memory accesses. Significant research focuses on taking advantage of data locality, resulting in many schemes that detect memory access patterns, so that cache eviction and insertion decisions can be determined by the reference predictions to reduce cache miss rate [8], [11], [13]..."

**APPLY:** The canonical problem should be named with a citation to the canonical paper that named it ("the well-known Memory Wall problem [1]"). The new workload or context should be named precisely (not "modern workloads" but "Large Language Model inference on CPUs"). The technical reason should be stated as a mechanism, not a symptom ("due to the quadratic memory complexity inherent in self-attention mechanisms").

---

**PARAGRAPH 2 — Prior Technique and Gap**

**RULE I2: Characterize the prior technique positively and broadly, with a citation sweep. Then pivot to the gap with "Although... we find" or "Despite... we observe."**

The prior technique deserves fair characterization before you identify its limitation. Introducing it negatively ("existing work is limited...") signals unfamiliarity with the literature and weakens your credibility. The positive characterization, backed by a citation sweep, demonstrates mastery.

**EVIDENCE (Zion):** "Hardware prefetching is a widely used technique to mitigate memory access latency by predicting and fetching data closer to the CPU before it is needed, thereby improving overall system performance [6]–[16]. Despite numerous proposed hardware prefetchers, we observe three critical limitations that constrain their effectiveness in memory-intensive workloads."

**EVIDENCE (CARE):** "Significant research focuses on taking advantage of data locality, resulting in many schemes that detect memory access patterns, so that cache eviction and insertion decisions can be determined by the reference predictions to reduce cache miss rate [8], [11], [13], [14], [15], [17], [18], [19], [20], [21], [23], [25], [26], [27], [33], [35], [36], [38], [39], [41], [45], [47], [48], [51], [53]. Although such locality-based cache management frameworks may reduce the number of misses, we find that considering both locality and concurrency can further improve the state-of-the-art locality-only optimizations."

**APPLY:** The citation sweep should include 6+ citations. It signals you have read the field, not just a handful of papers. The pivot phrase is either "Despite [N techniques], we observe [N] critical limitations" (Zion — used when the prior technique is fundamentally inadequate) or "Although [technique] may [X], we find that [Y] can further improve" (CARE — used when the prior technique is valid but incomplete).

---

**RULE I3: Pre-announce the count before enumerating limitations.**

This is the single most commonly violated rule in PhD student introductions. The count must appear before the first limitation is named. "We observe three critical limitations" — not "We observe the following limitations" and not letting the reader count to three themselves.

**EVIDENCE (Zion):** "Despite numerous proposed hardware prefetchers, we observe **three critical limitations** that constrain their effectiveness in memory-intensive workloads."

**EVIDENCE (CARE):** CARE does not enumerate numbered limitations in the introduction because its framing is additive (we find an additional opportunity) rather than adversarial (we find fundamental flaws). When using the CARE framing, the count is replaced by a directional claim: "we find that considering both locality and concurrency **can further improve**."

**APPLY:** If you find yourself writing "First... Second... Third..." without having pre-announced "three," go back and add the count. If a reviewer must count your bullets, you have failed this rule.

---

**RULE I4: Enumerate limitations as First/Second/Third, each with: statement → mechanism → consequence.**

Each limitation is three beats: what is wrong, why it is wrong, what the consequence is. A limitation that ends after the statement ("existing prefetchers lack adaptability") is weak; it describes a symptom without explaining the structure of the failure or its impact.

**EVIDENCE (Zion) — First limitation:** "First, memory-intensive workloads typically exhibit distinct and dynamic phases, each characterized by unique memory access behaviors. However, existing prefetchers often fail to adequately capture the diverse memory access patterns occurring across these varying phases, **thereby reducing overall pattern coverage**."

**EVIDENCE (Zion) — Second limitation:** "Second, most prefetchers lack the ability to utilize runtime feedback, **leading to** limited adaptability to system dynamics and thus suboptimal performance. Hardware prefetches without proper runtime control can introduce undesirable side effects such as increased memory bandwidth consumption, cache pollution, and memory access interference, **ultimately diminishing or even negating** the expected performance benefits."

**EVIDENCE (Zion) — Third limitation:** "Third, state-of-the-art prefetchers typically rely on a large, unified prediction table to maintain high accuracy, **incurring** excessive storage overhead, particularly at cache levels. Moreover, this unified prediction table **inherently suffers from** entry conflicts across unrelated data streams, causing premature eviction of locally frequent yet globally rare patterns..."

**APPLY:** The causal connectives are mandatory: "thereby," "leading to," "ultimately," "incurring," "inherently suffers from." These are not stylistic choices — they are logical signals that convert description into argument. Do not let a limitation paragraph end without a consequence.

---

**RULE I5: Close the limitation block with a pivot sentence that maps back to your three-adjective descriptor.**

The pivot sentence bridges the problem identification and the solution proposal. It must mirror the three-adjective descriptor from the abstract. This sentence confirms to the reviewer that the limitations are not an accident of framing — they directly motivate the system design.

**EVIDENCE (Zion):** "These limitations motivate the need for a **comprehensive, adaptive, and lightweight** hardware prefetcher for modern memory-intensive workloads."

**EVIDENCE (CARE):** *(CARE does not use this exact pivot because its limitations are not numbered. Instead, it pivots directly: "In this work, we first introduce and formally define the concept of Pure Miss Contribution (PMC).")*

**APPLY:** If you use the Zion (adversarial) framing, the pivot sentence is mandatory and must contain the three adjectives verbatim. If you use the CARE (additive) framing, you may pivot directly to the system proposal. Do not use both structures.

---

**PARAGRAPH 3 — The Hinge: Proposing the System**

**RULE I6: Introduce the system with a hinge sentence, then enumerate features that map to limitations.**

The hinge sentence is "To this end, we propose [SYSTEM], which incorporates the following core features:" It signals the transition from problem to solution. The features that follow must parallel the limitations that preceded them — one feature per limitation.

**EVIDENCE (Zion):** "To this end, we propose Zion, which incorporates the following core features to overcome existing limitations and effectively handle diverse and dynamic access patterns. First, at the core lies the ITSM (Independent Temporal-Spatial Modules) design... Second, Zion employs dynamic runtime adjustment... Finally, Zion achieves a balanced trade-off between performance and overhead..."

**EVIDENCE (CARE):** "In this work, we first introduce and formally define the concept of Pure Miss Contribution (PMC)... We then present CARE, a concurrency-aware cache management framework that takes both data locality and concurrency into account."

**APPLY:** The Zion pattern uses First/Second/Finally for features, explicitly mirroring the First/Second/Third of the limitations. This structural parallelism is not accidental — it is a signal to the reviewer that every limitation has been addressed. CARE's pattern is sequential narrative (first/then) because its contributions build on each other causally: the metric enables the framework.

---

**PARAGRAPH 4 — Evaluation**

**RULE I7: State evaluation scope (benchmarks + comparison systems), then "consistently outperforms named baselines," then "Notably, [specific peak number]."**

The evaluation paragraph in the introduction is not a teaser — it is a commitment. Name the benchmarks. Name the baselines. Give a real number. "Notably" is the marker for the peak result.

**EVIDENCE (Zion):** "The performance of Zion is extensively evaluated using memory-intensive workloads from the SPEC CPU2006 [17] and SPEC CPU2017 [18] benchmark suites, as well as diverse self-attention workloads from various LLM architectures [19]... The results demonstrate that Zion, with its comprehensive and adaptive design, consistently outperforms state-of-the-art (SOTA) hardware prefetchers, including IPCP [7], Bingo [9], SPP [6], and Berti [8], across a range of system configurations. Notably, in a 4-core configuration, Zion achieves performance improvements of 18.2%, 10.1%, 8.0% and 5.1% over these SOTAs, respectively in SPEC CPU [17], [18] workloads. Moreover, Zion improves the performance of self-attention workloads, up to 42.2% speedup over the baseline in an 8-core configuration."

**EVIDENCE (CARE):** "Our experimental results show that CARE outperforms state-of-the-art cache management schemes. Furthermore, CARE has low overhead and can be practically implemented in hardware."

**APPLY:** Zion's evaluation paragraph is the stronger model because it names specific baselines (IPCP, Bingo, SPP, Berti) and specific numbers (18.2%, 10.1%, 8.0%, 5.1%) with the word "respectively." CARE's introduction keeps numbers in the abstract only. For most venues, follow Zion's model: real numbers in the introduction signal a mature evaluation.

---

**PARAGRAPH 5 — Contributions**

**RULE I8: Use a fixed contribution list header. Never vary it.**

The header is a ritual phrase that reviewers use to locate contributions quickly during review. Deviating from the standard forms adds no information and makes the reviewer work harder.

**EVIDENCE (Zion):** "The contributions of this paper are summarized as follows:"

**EVIDENCE (CARE):** "To summarize, this paper makes the following contributions:"

**APPLY:** Use one of these two forms verbatim. Do not write "Our contributions are:" or "We make the following contributions:" or any other variant.

---

**RULE I9: Each contribution bullet starts with "We [active verb] [artifact]" and includes a relative clause or appositive that specifies what it does.**

Contribution bullets are claims, not process descriptions. The active verb names what you did; the relative clause names why it matters. The bullet cannot end on the verb — "We propose ITSM" is not a contribution bullet, it is a one-word summary.

**EVIDENCE (Zion):**
- "We comprehensively analyze the memory access patterns of memory-intensive workloads from SPEC CPU benchmarks and self-attention mechanisms, **identifying their diverse and irregular behaviors**."
- "We propose ITSM, a novel design based on independent temporal-spatial modules, **to enhance prediction accuracy and coverage of varying memory access patterns**."
- "We propose a runtime adaptive mechanism **that dynamically adjusts prefetch and insertion decisions based on real-time system feedback**."
- "We extensively evaluate and demonstrate that Zion **consistently outperforms SOTA prefetchers on memory-intensive workloads with lightweight overhead**."

**EVIDENCE (CARE):**
- "We introduce the pure miss contribution (PMC), a novel and accurate metric **to quantify the cost and performance impact of outstanding cache misses**. We describe how PMC can be measured in modern cache hierarchies. We find that PMC is predictable and can be used for cache optimization."
- "We present CARE, a comprehensive cache management framework that considers both locality and concurrency. CARE is general for all types of applications, practical with low hardware implementation overhead, and adaptive with a novel Dynamic Threshold Reconfiguration Mechanism (DTRM)."
- "Our evaluations show that CARE **substantially improves upon existing state-of-the-art cache management schemes over a wide variety of workloads in a wide range of system configurations** and performs well with data prefetching."

**APPLY:** CARE's bullets are longer than Zion's because CARE's contributions are more complex — each bullet carries its own sub-claims. The core pattern is the same: verb + artifact + what it does. Neither paper ends a bullet on the verb alone.

---

**RULE I10: Order contributions as: analysis/observation → design (1 or 2 bullets) → evaluation.**

This ordering is not arbitrary. Analysis first establishes that the problem is real; design second shows how you solved it; evaluation third provides the evidence. Reversing this order — leading with evaluation, then design — reads as overclaiming.

**EVIDENCE (Zion):** (1) analysis of memory access patterns → (2) ITSM design → (3) runtime adaptive mechanism → (4) evaluation demonstrating consistent outperformance.

**EVIDENCE (CARE):** (1) PMC metric introduction (analysis + insight) → (2) CARE framework design → (3) evaluation substantially improving upon state-of-the-art.

**APPLY:** When you have one design contribution, your list is three bullets: analysis, design, evaluation. When you have two design contributions, your list is four bullets: analysis, design A, design B, evaluation.

---

**RULE I11: The introduction ends on the final contribution bullet. No roadmap paragraph.**

"The rest of this paper is organized as follows: Section 2 presents background..." is banned. It is the most reliable signal that a paper was written by a student who ran out of ideas for the introduction. Reviewers have seen the roadmap paragraph thousands of times and skip it; its presence signals that the author did not trust the reader to navigate the paper.

**EVIDENCE (Zion):** The introduction ends immediately after the final bullet: "We extensively evaluate and demonstrate that Zion consistently outperforms SOTA prefetchers on memory-intensive workloads with lightweight overhead."

**EVIDENCE (CARE):** The introduction ends immediately after the final numbered contribution.

**APPLY:** Delete the roadmap paragraph. If you are worried about navigation, put it in the related work or background section — or nowhere, because the section headers are navigation enough.

### Sentence-Level Grammar

These are the construction patterns that appear consistently across both papers. They are not suggestions — they are the craft decisions that distinguish publishable prose from student drafts.

---

**RULE SG1: Claims come before evidence. Always.**

**EVIDENCE (Zion):** "We observe three critical limitations..." — the claim (three limitations) appears before the enumeration.

**EVIDENCE (CARE):** "We find that considering both locality and concurrency can further improve the state-of-the-art locality-only optimizations." — the claim (improvement is possible) appears before the mechanism explanation.

**APPLY:** If you find yourself writing "Table 3 shows that..." or "As seen in Figure 4..." you have inverted the order. Rewrite as: "System X achieves Y% improvement (Table 3)." or "Pattern Z appears in 94% of traces (Figure 4)."

---

**RULE SG2: Make causal connectives explicit. Do not imply causality — state it.**

Both papers use a consistent set of causal connectives: "leading to," "resulting in," "thereby," "ultimately," "incurring," "causing," "thus."

**EVIDENCE (Zion):** "...their limited adaptability **leads to** excessive storage overhead and reduced effectiveness..." / "...existing prefetchers often fail to adequately capture the diverse memory access patterns occurring across these varying phases, **thereby reducing** overall pattern coverage." / "...Hardware prefetches without proper runtime control can introduce undesirable side effects..., **ultimately diminishing or even negating** the expected performance benefits."

**EVIDENCE (CARE):** "The performance loss resulting from a cache miss can be hidden by access overlapping. **Thus**, a more accurate cost metric for cache misses may help improve cache performance further..."

**APPLY:** After every limitation or consequence sentence, check for a causal connective. If you wrote "X is a problem. Y is bad." with no explicit link, add one: "X is a problem, **leading to** Y."

---

**RULE SG3: Use "Although X, we find Y" for gap claims. Do not use "However" as a paragraph opener.**

"However" as a paragraph opener is the most overused transition in systems papers. It signals a reversal but does not specify the relationship. The "Although X, we find Y" pattern makes the contrast explicit in a single sentence and forces you to complete both halves of the argument.

**EVIDENCE (Zion):** *(Zion uses "Despite... we observe" rather than "Although... we find")*: "Despite numerous proposed hardware prefetchers, we observe three critical limitations..."

**EVIDENCE (CARE):** "**Although** such locality-based cache management frameworks may reduce the number of misses, **we find that** considering both locality and concurrency can further improve the state-of-the-art locality-only optimizations."

**APPLY:** Reserve "However" for mid-paragraph pivots within a single argument, not for opening a new logical block. For transitions between prior work and your contribution, use "Despite [X], we observe [Y]" or "Although [X], we find [Y]."

---

**RULE SG4: Use active voice for system actions. Never write "[system] tries to" or "[system] aims to."**

Active voice is not a style preference in systems papers — it is a correctness requirement. A system does not try to do something; it does it, or it fails. "Aims to" and "tries to" hedge the claim and undercut the contribution.

**EVIDENCE (Zion):** "Zion **uses** Independent Temporal-Spatial Modules." / "Zion **leverages** runtime feedback." / "Zion **employs** dynamic runtime adjustment." / "Zion **achieves** a balanced trade-off."

**EVIDENCE (CARE):** "CARE **learns** the re-reference behavior and PMC value of each miss access." / "CARE **augments** existing cache insertion and hit-promotion policies." / "CARE **outperforms** state-of-the-art cache management schemes."

**APPLY:** Search your draft for "aims to," "tries to," "is designed to," "is intended to," "seeks to." Every occurrence is a bug. Replace with the active verb: "achieves," "provides," "reduces," "outperforms."

---

**RULE SG5: Use intensifiers "significantly," "consistently," "inherently," "particularly." Never "very" or "quite."**

**EVIDENCE (Zion):** "state-of-the-art prefetchers typically rely on a large, unified prediction table to maintain high accuracy, **incurring excessive** storage overhead" / "this unified prediction table **inherently** suffers from entry conflicts" / "Zion **consistently** outperforms state-of-the-art prefetchers"

**EVIDENCE (CARE):** "CARE **substantially** improves upon existing state-of-the-art cache management schemes" / "cache management schemes are designed to **significantly** improve performance" / "the shared LLC can observe **very** mixed access patterns" *(the one instance of "very" in CARE appears in the background section, not in the introduction or abstract — avoid it in those sections)*

**APPLY:** "Very" and "quite" add no information; they dilute the assertion. "Significantly" is acceptable only when accompanied by a number (otherwise it is equally hollow). "Consistently," "inherently," and "particularly" carry specific semantic weight and are not interchangeable.

---

**RULE SG6: Use specific problem verbs. Use specific contribution verbs. Never mix them.**

Problem verbs signal failure. Contribution verbs signal delivery. Mixing them ("the system aims to struggle with...") is a category error.

**Problem verbs:** "struggle," "fail," "lack," "suffer from," "constrain," "limit," "reduce," "diminish," "negate," "incur"

**Contribution verbs:** "propose," "present," "introduce," "demonstrate," "achieve," "outperform," "improve," "reduce," "show"

**EVIDENCE (Zion) — problem:** "existing prefetchers often **fail** to adequately capture..." / "most prefetchers **lack** the ability to utilize runtime feedback" / "this unified prediction table **inherently suffers from** entry conflicts"

**EVIDENCE (Zion) — contribution:** "We **propose** Zion..." / "We **extensively evaluate and demonstrate**..." / "Zion **consistently outperforms**..."

**EVIDENCE (CARE) — problem:** "utilizing data locality to enhance cache performance **becomes more and more difficult**" / "MLP-based cost can identify costly misses... However, we **find** that hit-miss overlapping impacts the cost of misses as well"

**EVIDENCE (CARE) — contribution:** "We **introduce** the pure miss contribution (PMC)..." / "We **present** CARE..." / "CARE **substantially improves** upon..."

**APPLY:** Do a pass over your introduction and mark every sentence as either "problem" or "contribution." Confirm that problem verbs appear only in the problem section and contribution verbs appear only in the proposal and contribution sections.

### Vocabulary Catalog

#### Required Words (both papers use these; reviewers expect them)

| Word/Phrase | Where it appears | Usage |
|---|---|---|
| `consistently outperforms` | abstract, evaluation paragraph | Required in both; describes cross-configuration dominance |
| `extensively` | abstract, evaluation paragraph | "extensively evaluated" — signals methodological rigor |
| `state-of-the-art` | abstract sentence 2, evaluation | Describes the comparison class; must be named, not generic |
| `We propose` | abstract sentence 4, hinge sentence | The canonical contribution verb |
| `We observe` | limitations paragraph | Marks empirical findings; pre-announces limitation count |
| `To this end` | hinge sentence | The canonical transition from problem to solution |
| `inherently` | third limitation | Marks a structural (not incidental) flaw |
| `thereby` | limitation consequence | Causal connective for mechanical consequences |
| `leading to` | limitation consequence | Causal connective for systemic consequences |
| `ultimately` | multi-step consequence | Causal connective for end-state consequences |
| `respectively` | numbers sentence | Required when presenting parallel results |
| `Notably` | evaluation paragraph peak result | Marks the headline number |
| `comprehensively` / `comprehensive` | analysis contribution bullet | Signals full coverage of the problem space |
| `dynamic` | mechanism description | Signals runtime adaptability |
| `lightweight` | descriptor or contribution | Signals practicality and implementability |
| `concurrency-aware` / `adaptive` | descriptor | Domain-specific adjectives that differentiate from prior work |

#### Forbidden Words (both papers avoid; reviewers penalize)

| Forbidden word | Why it is forbidden | Replacement |
|---|---|---|
| `may` | hedges; implies uncertainty about your own results | Replace with the result: "improves by X%" |
| `could` | hedges | Replace with "does" or give the number |
| `might` | hedges | Same as "could" |
| `potentially` | hedges | Delete entirely; if uncertain, scope the claim instead |
| `we believe` | hedges and uses first-person uncertainty | Replace with "We show" or "We find" |
| `it is worth noting` | content-free filler | Delete; make the point directly |
| `very` | vacuous intensifier | Delete; if you need emphasis, provide a number |
| `quite` | vacuous intensifier | Delete entirely |
| `significant` (without a number) | means nothing without quantification | Either give the number or use "substantial" |
| `novel` | every paper claims novelty; the claim is uninformative | Let the contribution bullet describe what is new |
| `promising` | is an aspiration, not a result | Replace with the actual result |
| `In this section, we...` | content-free roadmap | Delete entirely; open with the section's conclusion |
| `The rest of this paper is organized as follows` | roadmap paragraph; banned | Delete entirely |
| `leverage` | academic filler when used generically | Use "use," "employ," or a more specific verb |
| `utilize` | academic filler | Use "use" |
| `paradigm` | academic filler | Use the specific technical term |

### Common Failure Modes

These are the recurring errors found in PhD student drafts before advisor review. Each failure mode has a diagnosis and a fix.

---

**FAILURE 1: The abstract opens with "In this paper, we propose..."**

*Diagnosis:* The author began writing at the proposal rather than at the problem. The reader has no context for why the proposal matters.

*Symptom in review:* "The motivation for this work is unclear."

*Fix:* Add the opening sentence (Rule A1) before the proposal sentence. The proposal sentence becomes sentence 4, not sentence 1.

---

**FAILURE 2: The abstract hedges — "may improve," "can potentially reduce," "we believe our system offers..."**

*Diagnosis:* The author is uncertain about the results or is attempting to avoid overclaiming. This is a legitimate concern resolved by scoping, not by hedging.

*Symptom in review:* "The authors seem unsure of their own contributions."

*Fix:* Replace every hedged verb with its result. "May improve performance by 10%" becomes "improves performance by 10%." If you do not have the number, leave the sentence structure but insert a placeholder for the final number.

---

**FAILURE 3: The introduction enumerates limitations without pre-announcing the count.**

*Diagnosis:* The author wrote "First... Second... Third..." without writing "we observe three critical limitations" first. The reader must count.

*Symptom in review:* "The paper would benefit from a clearer problem statement."

*Fix:* Insert the count sentence before the first limitation. "Despite [N technique], we observe [N] critical limitations that [effect]."

---

**FAILURE 4: Limitation bullets end on the statement without a mechanism or consequence.**

*Diagnosis:* "First, existing prefetchers lack adaptability." This is a symptom, not an argument.

*Symptom in review:* "The limitations section is superficial" or "It is not clear why these limitations are fundamental."

*Fix:* Add mechanism: *why* does the limitation exist? Then add consequence: *what does it cost?* Use the causal connectives: "leading to," "thereby," "ultimately."

---

**FAILURE 5: The contribution bullets are process descriptions, not claims.**

*Diagnosis:* "We propose a system that..." or "We implement a mechanism for..." — these describe what you did, not what you showed.

*Symptom in review:* "The contributions are not clearly stated" or "It is unclear what the paper demonstrates."

*Fix:* Add the relative clause or appositive that completes the claim. "We propose ITSM" becomes "We propose ITSM, a novel design based on independent temporal-spatial modules, **to enhance prediction accuracy and coverage of varying memory access patterns**."

---

**FAILURE 6: The introduction ends with a roadmap paragraph.**

*Diagnosis:* "The rest of this paper is organized as follows: Section 2 presents background, Section 3 describes the design..."

*Symptom in review:* *(Reviewers do not complain about roadmap paragraphs; they simply skip them. The damage is subtler: the paragraph signals that the author ran out of ideas and filled space.)*

*Fix:* Delete the paragraph entirely. The introduction ends on the final contribution bullet.

---

**FAILURE 7: The three-adjective descriptor in the abstract does not match the feature enumeration in the introduction.**

*Diagnosis:* Abstract says "comprehensive, adaptive, and lightweight." Introduction enumerates features for "coverage, accuracy, and efficiency" — different words, unclear mapping.

*Symptom in review:* "The relationship between the design components is not clear."

*Fix:* Align the three adjectives in the abstract with the three features in the introduction. The pivot sentence ("These limitations motivate the need for a [comprehensive, adaptive, and lightweight] prefetcher") is the bridge — it must use the same three words that appear in both the abstract and the feature enumeration.

---

**FAILURE 8: Evaluation paragraph in the introduction omits specific baseline names.**

*Diagnosis:* "Zion consistently outperforms state-of-the-art prefetchers." Which ones? The reviewer cannot evaluate the claim without knowing who you beat.

*Symptom in review:* "The evaluation comparison is not rigorous" or "It is unclear what the baselines are."

*Fix:* Name every baseline by its published name with its citation: "including IPCP [7], Bingo [9], SPP [6], and Berti [8]."

---

**FAILURE 9: Numbers appear in the abstract but not in the introduction, or vice versa.**

*Diagnosis:* The author put all numbers in the abstract and left the introduction's evaluation paragraph qualitative. Or the opposite: detailed numbers in the introduction, none in the abstract.

*Symptom in review:* Inconsistency between abstract and body, or reviewers cannot locate the headline result.

*Fix:* The abstract's final numbers and the introduction's evaluation paragraph should be consistent but not identical. The abstract gives peak numbers (e.g., "up to 43.2%"). The introduction gives per-configuration breakdowns (e.g., "18.2%, 10.1%, 8.0% and 5.1% over these SOTAs, respectively"). Both sections should contain numbers.

---

**FAILURE 10: Passive voice for system actions.**

*Diagnosis:* "Predictions are generated by the ITSM module." / "Cache decisions are guided by the PMC metric."

*Symptom in review:* Prose reads as a laboratory report rather than a system paper.

*Fix:* "The ITSM module generates predictions." / "The PMC metric guides cache decisions." Search for "is [verb]ed by" and invert every occurrence.

---

*End of Chapter 1.*

## Chapter 2: Background & Related Work

*A style guide chapter for systems and architecture PhD students. All rules are derived forensically from two accepted papers: Zion (MICRO) and CARE (TPDS). Direct quotes appear exactly as extracted from the published texts.*

---

### Section Architecture: Two Models

Every systems paper must decide whether to write a fused Background+Related Work section or separate them. This is not a stylistic preference — it is a structural decision driven by the novelty argument and the paper's dependence on empirical motivation. Both models are correct in the right context. The wrong choice weakens the paper's logic.

---

#### Model A: The Fusion Model (Zion)

Zion fuses everything into a single section titled **"BACKGROUND AND MOTIVATION"** with two subsections: (A) prior systems, (B) empirical characterization of the workload. There is no standalone Related Work section anywhere in the paper.

**Why this works for Zion:** The paper's core claim is that prior systems fail on a class of workloads (memory-intensive SPEC + self-attention). The empirical characterization is not merely context — it is the proof of inadequacy. Separating prior work from the motivation data would sever the logical chain. By fusing them, the paper says: here is what exists, here is what the workload actually does, and therefore here is why everything existing is wrong. The reader cannot dispute the motivation because the evidence is adjacent to the claim.

**When to use Model A:**
- Your paper's motivation depends on empirical data (traces, measurements, profiling).
- The flaws of prior work can only be understood after seeing the workload characterization.
- You have a compact prior-work landscape (4–6 systems).
- The venue expects short page counts (workshop papers, 6-page conference papers).

**When NOT to use Model A:**
- The prior-work landscape is large and diverse (more than 8 distinct system families).
- A reviewer unfamiliar with the domain needs a tutorial before they can evaluate your design.
- Your novelty is orthogonal to prior work rather than a direct response to its failures.

---

#### Model B: The Layered Model (CARE)

CARE separates sections into **"BACKGROUND AND PRELIMINARIES"** (Section II, four subsections: MLP, C-AMAT model, locality-based cache management, cost-based cache management) and a separate **"MOTIVATION"** section (Section III, two subsections: limitations of locality-based schemes, limitations of MLP-based schemes). Related work is embedded as the final two subsections of Background (Sections II-C and II-D), each organized by functional category.

**Why this works for CARE:** The paper introduces a new metric (PMC) that requires the reader to understand three antecedent concepts — MLP, C-AMAT, and existing management paradigms — before the motivation can land. The Background section functions as a progressive technical tutorial. Each subsection ends with an implicit gap that the next subsection fills. By the time the reader reaches Section III (Motivation), they have all the vocabulary and can follow the argument without re-reading.

**When to use Model B:**
- Your paper introduces a new concept that requires understanding two or more existing concepts first.
- You are writing for a broad audience that may not know all prerequisite terminology.
- The prior-work landscape spans multiple functional categories (locality-based, cost-based, ML-based, etc.).
- The venue is a journal (TPDS, TOCS) where longer Background sections are expected.

---

#### Decision Rule (One Sentence)

If the empirical data IS the proof of prior-work failure, fuse (Model A). If the reader needs conceptual prerequisites BEFORE they can understand why prior work fails, separate (Model B).

---

### Background Subsection Blueprint

Every background subsection, regardless of model, follows the same internal architecture. The five-sentence paragraph structure below applies to every non-trivial concept introduced.

---

#### The Five-Sentence Paragraph

**Sentence 1 — Behavioral claim (declarative, present tense).** Do not start with the technique name. Start with the phenomenon that the technique addresses.

**RULE**: The first sentence of every background subsection makes a claim about observable system behavior, not about a technique.

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "Multi-core and multi-threading designs, as well as advanced caching techniques, increase data access concurrency. As a result, a number of memory accesses can concurrently coexist in the memory hierarchy."

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "Memory-intensive SPEC Workloads. We analyze memory-intensive SPEC workloads and identify two critical challenges in SOTA prefetcher designs."

**APPLY**: Write the phenomenon first ("X occurs in modern systems"), then introduce the metric or technique that addresses it.

---

**Sentence 2 — Mechanism.** Once the phenomenon is named, explain the mechanism that causes or measures it.

**RULE**: Name the technique only after establishing why it exists. Use present tense for the mechanism description.

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "*Memory Level Parallelism (MLP)* can be used to measure miss concurrency. MLP captures the number of outstanding cache misses that can be generated and executed in an overlapped manner."

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "Hardware prefetching is a widely adopted technique for mitigating memory access latency by proactively fetching data before it is requested."

**APPLY**: One sentence for the phenomenon; one sentence for the mechanism. Do not combine them.

---

**Sentence 3 — Concrete consequence.** Name a specific behavior or boundary condition that makes the mechanism matter.

**RULE**: After the mechanism, give a concrete consequence. Use "Some...while some..." or "the more...the smaller..." to show the range of outcomes.

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "Some misses are isolated, while some occur concurrently with other misses. The more cache misses occur concurrently, the smaller the impact of each cache miss on performance since all concurrent misses will amortize the total memory stall cycles."

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "For instance, in memory-intensive workloads such as mcf, we observe two distinct phases. The first phase involves high latencies between consecutive memory accesses, characterized by significant IP variation and relatively small address deltas."

**APPLY**: Use a concrete example workload or configuration. Avoid abstract generalizations ("this can cause problems") without grounding in a specific observable behavior.

---

**Sentence 4 — Quantitative boundary.** Give a number. A sentence without a number at this position is a missed opportunity.

**RULE**: Every concept introduction should include at least one quantitative bound.

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "In all benchmarks, 30% to 80% misses have hit-miss overlapping."

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "The average MPKI values for the L1 data cache (L1D), L2 cache (L2C), and last-level cache (LLC) are 36.2, 11.6 and 10.9, respectively. Moreover, we observe that 94.0% of accesses that miss in the L2 also miss in the LLC."

**APPLY**: Run the measurements before writing the section. If you do not have a number, the subsection is not ready to be written.

---

**Sentence 5 — Forward implication.** Close with a sentence that either names the gap ("However, X. Therefore, we need Y.") or forwards to your design ("In this work, we present X, which is inspired by Y.").

**RULE**: Background subsections must end with either a gap statement or a forward pointer. Never end on the description of prior art.

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "MLP-based cost can identify costly misses by considering the miss-miss overlapping. However, we find that hit-miss overlapping impacts the cost of misses as well, and modern memory systems have a lot of such overlapping accesses. Therefore, we need a holistic metric that is able to catch all types of overlapping and provide a better memory performance optimization guidance."

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "In this work, we present pure miss contribution metric (details in Section IV), which is inspired by C-AMAT model, and it shows the great potential to be incorporated with cache optimization frameworks."

**APPLY**: The gap statement formula is: "However, [limitation of current approach]. Therefore, we need [property your design will provide]." The forward pointer formula is: "In this work, we present [your design], which is inspired by [the concept just explained]."

---

#### Paired Contrast Pattern

**RULE**: Use "On the other hand" to complete a positive/negative pair within a paragraph. Both sides bound the concept being introduced.

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "Active pure miss cycles are the cycles that only contain the pure miss cycles (cycles 5 to 7 in Figure 1), and these cycles cause more performance degradation. On the other hand, the active non-pure miss cycles do not introduce heavy degradation, as the miss access cycle is overlapped and hidden."

**APPLY**: Whenever you define a binary partition (pure/non-pure, isolated/concurrent, frequent/rare), give one sentence for each side. The reader needs both poles to understand the concept.

---

#### Observation Callout Format (Zion Model)

For empirical characterization subsections, Zion uses numbered observation callouts followed by a supporting paragraph.

**RULE**: Format empirical findings as labeled observations ("Observation N.") with a full declarative sentence in bold or a box. The supporting paragraph provides the evidence. Close with "These observations highlight..." to signal the design implication.

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**:
> "Observation 1. Self-attention in LLMs exhibits very high MPKI rates across all levels of the cache hierarchy."
>
> "The quadratic memory complexity of the self-attention mechanism has a significant impact on the memory hierarchy behavior of LLMs."

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**:
> "Observation 2. Self-attention in LLMs exhibits a diverse and dynamic behavior in memory access patterns."
>
> "These observations highlight the importance of an adaptive prefetcher that tunes its decisions and insertion destinations at runtime to maintain efficiency without overwhelming the memory hierarchy."

**APPLY**: The observation callout is a claim, not a description. Write it as a strong declarative sentence with no hedging. The supporting paragraph is pure evidence. The closing "These observations highlight..." sentence is the design derivation connector.

---

#### Derivation Connector

**RULE**: Use "Therefore, based on [concept], [design decision]" to make design derivation explicit. Do not leave the reader to infer the logical step.

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "Therefore, based on the MLP concept, isolated misses are considered to hurt performance more than concurrent misses."

**APPLY**: Every time you move from an observation to a design property, use "Therefore, based on [X], [Y]." This sentence is the load-bearing joint between Background and Design.

---

### How to Introduce a Prior System

Every prior system introduced in a Background or Related Work section must follow the **Mandatory Four-Move structure**. Every move is required. Omitting any move creates a weak paragraph.

---

#### The Four-Move Template

**Move 1: NAME [citation] + active verb of mechanism**

**RULE**: Cite the system at first mention, immediately after the name, using a bracket citation. Use an active verb that names the mechanism, not a passive construction.

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "IPCP [7] classifies instruction pointers into categories: constant stride, complex stride, and global stream, enabling tailored prefetching strategies for each pattern type."

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "Bingo [9] enhances spatial pattern learning by associating short-range (IP-based) and long-range (IP plus memory region) access correlations."

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "Berti [8] tracks local deltas to generate timely and precise prefetches."

**APPLY**: Mechanism verbs: "classifies", "enhances", "predicts", "tracks", "learns", "augments". NEVER use "proposes", "achieves", "introduces". Those verbs describe the paper, not the system.

---

**Move 2: ARCHITECTURE detail + specific size in parentheses**

**RULE**: Include a specific implementation detail — typically a table size, number of bits, or structural choice — in parentheses after the component name.

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "Bingo utilizes a large (114KB) unified hardware table to accommodate and manage multiple prediction streams."

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "it maintains two compact history tables (totaling 19.85KB), resulting in moderate storage overhead."

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "IPCP employs a unified IP (instruction pointer) table shared among these categories."

**APPLY**: If you cannot name the implementation detail, you have not read the paper carefully enough. The specific number (114KB, 19.85KB) is not decoration — it anchors the tradeoff described in Move 4.

---

**Move 3: BENEFIT**

**RULE**: State the positive contribution directly and briefly. Often a participial phrase ("improving performance", "resulting in moderate storage overhead"). Never expand this to more than one clause.

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "improving performance at the cost of significantly increased storage overhead" — here the benefit ("improving performance") and the cost are combined in one phrase. The cost comes after "at the cost of."

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "resulting in moderate storage overhead" — used as the benefit (compact size) for Berti.

**APPLY**: Keep the benefit to a phrase, not a sentence. The flaw in Move 4 will naturally contrast with it.

---

**Move 4: FLAW (led by "However" or "at the cost of")**

**RULE**: Every prior system paragraph ends with its limitation. Use "However" as the pivot word. Never use "unfortunately", "sadly", or "regrettably". Use "at the cost of" for tradeoff framing. Use "which can cause [flaw] due to [mechanism]" when the failure mode has an identifiable mechanism.

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "IPCP employs a unified IP (instruction pointer) table shared among these categories, which can cause early evictions of infrequent but potentially valuable entries due to conflicts."

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "Bingo utilizes a large (114KB) unified hardware table to accommodate and manage multiple prediction streams, improving performance at the cost of significantly increased storage overhead."

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "However, its count-based confidence mechanism inherently underestimates entries." [SPP]

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "However, its count-based confidence mechanism ignores rare but accurate stream patterns in favor of frequent deltas reducing overall effectiveness." [Berti]

**APPLY**: The flaw must connect to the architecture detail in Move 2. If you named the table size, the flaw should involve that table size. If you named the confidence mechanism, the flaw should involve that mechanism. The connection between Move 2 and Move 4 is the proof that you understand the system, not just its result.

---

#### Complete Four-Move Example

The following paragraph from Zion demonstrates all four moves in sequence:

> "Bingo [9] enhances spatial pattern learning by associating short-range (IP-based) and long-range (IP plus memory region) access correlations. [Move 1] Bingo utilizes a large (114KB) unified hardware table to accommodate and manage multiple prediction streams, [Move 2] improving performance [Move 3] at the cost of significantly increased storage overhead." [Move 4]

And another:

> "Berti [8] tracks local deltas to generate timely and precise prefetches. [Move 1] By associating first IP accessing a page with local deltas of that page, it maintains two compact history tables (totaling 19.85KB), resulting in moderate storage overhead. [Move 2+3] However, its count-based confidence mechanism ignores rare but accurate stream patterns in favor of frequent deltas reducing overall effectiveness." [Move 4]

---

#### One System Per Paragraph

**RULE**: Each prior system gets exactly one paragraph. Never combine two systems in one paragraph.

**APPLY**: If you find yourself writing "Similarly, System B..." within the same paragraph as System A, stop. The reader needs the boundary between systems to be explicit. Start a new paragraph for every new system.

---

#### Citation Placement

**RULE**: Bracket citation goes immediately after the system name, not at the end of the sentence.

**CORRECT**: "IPCP [7] classifies instruction pointers..."
**INCORRECT**: "IPCP classifies instruction pointers into categories [7]."

**APPLY**: The citation-after-name pattern signals "this is the paper I am describing." An end-of-sentence citation can mean anything — it might be a methodology reference, a previous result, or context support. For system introductions, always cite after the name.

---

### How to Critique Without Being Dismissive

The flaw vocabulary in systems architecture writing is highly constrained. Reviewers read hundreds of papers and recognize non-standard criticism as either aggressive (which reads as arrogant) or vague (which reads as lazy). The phrases below are exact formulas taken from accepted papers.

---

#### Flaw Vocabulary Catalog

**Formula 1: Mechanism-driven failure**
> "which can cause [flaw] due to [mechanism]"

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "which can cause early evictions of infrequent but potentially valuable entries due to conflicts"

**APPLY**: Use this when the failure mode is mechanistically traceable. The "due to [mechanism]" clause proves you understand why the failure occurs, not just that it occurs.

---

**Formula 2: Tradeoff framing**
> "at the cost of [overhead]"

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "improving performance at the cost of significantly increased storage overhead"

**APPLY**: Use "at the cost of" when the system does achieve its goal but at a price that makes it unsuitable for your setting. This is the most respectful critique formula — it acknowledges the benefit before naming the cost.

---

**Formula 3: Inherent structural limitation**
> "inherently [suffers from / underestimates / overestimates]"

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "its count-based confidence mechanism inherently underestimates entries"

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "this unified prediction table inherently suffers from entry conflicts across unrelated data streams"

**APPLY**: "Inherently" signals that the limitation is not a bug but a structural consequence of the design choice. Use this only when you can demonstrate that no patch can fix the problem without abandoning the core mechanism.

---

**Formula 4: Selective ignorance**
> "ignores [X] in favor of [Y]"

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "its count-based confidence mechanism ignores rare but accurate stream patterns in favor of frequent deltas"

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "MLP does not consider hit-miss overlapping. Therefore, the MLP-aware replacement policy can be further improved."

**APPLY**: Use this when the prior system is not wrong, just incomplete. "Ignores X in favor of Y" encodes a design tradeoff, not a mistake. This is the appropriate formula when the prior work and your work are not in head-to-head opposition but are addressing different subsets of the problem.

---

**Formula 5: Premature eviction**
> "prematurely evict[s] infrequent yet important entries"

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "traditional unified-table designs often prematurely evict infrequent yet important entries, particularly if a count-based confidence mechanism is employed"

**APPLY**: Use this exact phrase when the failure mode involves evicting the wrong entries from a fixed-size structure. The word "prematurely" is important — it signals timing failure, not just selection failure.

---

**Formula 6: Intensified flaw**
> "particularly if [worst-case condition]"

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "particularly if a count-based confidence mechanism is employed"

**APPLY**: Use this to sharpen the critique. The general failure + the specific worst case = a precise, testable claim. A reviewer can verify "particularly if" claims, which makes the critique credible.

---

**Formula 7: Measured limitation (not polemical)**
> "[X] may not necessarily lead to better overall performance"
> "[X] does not consider [Y]"

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "we pinpoint that reducing the miss rate may not necessarily lead to better overall performance"

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "MLP does not consider hit-miss overlapping"

**APPLY**: These are the most measured critique formulas. They are appropriate for the closest competitor (the system you build on). "May not necessarily" acknowledges that the prior system often does work — it just fails in the specific regime you are studying. Use this to distinguish your critique from a rejection of prior work.

---

#### Contrastive Pairs

**RULE**: Use 3-word contrastive pairs to encode design gaps. These pairs compress an argument into a phrase.

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**:
- "infrequent yet important" (entries that are rare but should be kept)
- "locally frequent yet globally rare" (patterns that dominate locally but are swamped globally)
- "rare but accurate" (predictions that are infrequent but correct)

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**:
- "isolated misses" vs "concurrent misses" (the core binary that motivates MLP)
- "locality and concurrency" (the two properties CARE unifies)

**APPLY**: Identify the core tension in the prior system's design and compress it into a contrastive pair. The pair should appear in the flaw sentence and should echo in the Abstract and Conclusion as the core motivation for your work.

---

#### What Never to Write

The following phrases appear in rejected drafts, not in accepted papers.

- "Unfortunately, this approach has significant drawbacks." — Vague. Name the specific drawback.
- "Sadly, prior work does not address our problem." — The word "sadly" has no place in a technical paper.
- "Previous work fails to..." — "Fails to" is too absolute. Use "does not consider" or "may not necessarily."
- "None of the existing approaches can handle..." — This claim requires exhaustive proof. Use "existing approaches focus on X and do not address Y."
- "X is naive." — Aggressive. The correct move is to identify the condition under which X breaks.

---

### Related Work Organization

Related Work sections must be organized by functional category, not chronologically. Chronological organization signals that the author is cataloguing prior work rather than arguing that their design occupies a distinct position.

---

#### Category Opening Formula

**RULE**: Open each related work category with a general characterization of the category before naming any specific system. The opening sentence names the mechanism, not the systems.

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "Several replacement studies are designed based on the re-reference prediction of cache blocks, determining the lifetime of the blocks in the cache."

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "Unlike locality-based cache management schemes that focus on reducing cache misses, several works improve cache performance by selectively eliminating expensive misses."

**APPLY**: The opening sentence tells the reader what unifies the category. After this sentence, cite 3–6 papers as a range to establish that the category is populated. Then introduce specific systems.

---

#### Category Breadth Citation

**RULE**: After the category opener, cite a range of papers (3–6) to signal that the category is established, not niche. Use a bracket range [X]–[Y] for this list.

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "management studies have focused on exploring prediction-based schemes to reduce the number of cache misses [17], [19], [40], [42], [45], [48]."

**APPLY**: The range citation is not a claim about any individual paper — it is a claim that the category exists. Individual citation comes only when you are discussing a specific system.

---

#### Closest Competitor Treatment

**RULE**: The system your design builds on or most directly competes with gets 4–5 sentences: observation that motivates it, mechanism, implementation details, limitation.

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "Jain and Lin propose Hawkeye [17]. Hawkeye simulates and learns Belady's optimal solution for a long history of memory accesses to predict the re-reference characteristic of future accesses. Hawkeye formulates the re-reference prediction as a binary prediction problem. If the incoming block is predicted to be 'cache-friendly', it will be inserted with a high priority. Otherwise, 'cache-averse' blocks will be marked as eviction candidates."

**APPLY**: This is the system the reader will most readily ask "why not just use X?" Your treatment of the closest competitor must be thorough enough to preempt that question.

---

#### Genealogy Framing

**RULE**: Use "Following in the footsteps of [X], [Y] does [Z]" to show intellectual lineage. This validates both systems while positioning yours in the family tree.

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "Following in the footsteps of Hawkeye, Mockingjay [41] mimics Belady's optimal solution effectively and introduces a cache replacement policy based on multi-class re-reference prediction."

**APPLY**: Genealogy framing is appropriate for 2–3 consecutive systems that build on each other. It compresses the history without requiring individual paragraph treatment for every system.

---

#### Minor Competitor Treatment

**RULE**: Minor competitors get 1–2 sentences: NAME [citation] + mechanism verb + brief description. No flaw required unless directly relevant to your paper's gap.

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "SRRIP [19] statically predicts an 'intermediate' re-reference interval at cache insertion time and updates the re-reference prediction on subsequent accesses. DRRIP [19] is proposed to improve performance by selecting the inserting position among different policies."

**APPLY**: Do not give every paper equal treatment. The depth of treatment is a signal to the reader about how important that prior work is to understanding your contribution.

---

#### Category-Closing Positioning Statement

**RULE**: Close each related work category block with a sentence that positions your design relative to the category. Use "Our design focuses on [X], which is more [Y] than the prior category."

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "Our design focuses on efficiency and lightweight, which is more practical to be implemented on latency-critical cache hierarchy without any pre-processing or training overhead."

**APPLY**: This is the thesis statement of your related work. Every category should end with one sentence that tells the reader why, given that category, your design is still necessary.

---

#### Critique Pattern for Related Work

**RULE**: Use the three-part critique pattern: positive capability → gap → design implication.

**Formula**: "[X] can [positive]. However, we find that [X does not consider Y]. Therefore, we need [Z]."

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "MLP-based cost can identify costly misses by considering the miss-miss overlapping. However, we find that hit-miss overlapping impacts the cost of misses as well, and modern memory systems have a lot of such overlapping accesses. Therefore, we need a holistic metric that is able to catch all types of overlapping."

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "While simple, the cost estimation model of LACS is not cycle-accurate, it is impossible to estimate the penalty of the misses on performance accurately."

**APPLY**: The three-part critique pattern closes off the "why not X" question. After reading this pattern, the reviewer understands both what X does well and exactly why X is insufficient for your problem.

---

#### No Standalone Transitions Between Subsections

**RULE**: Never write a standalone transition sentence ("In the next section, we discuss..."). The gap statement at the end of each subsection serves as the implicit transition.

**APPLY**: If you have written a sentence that only announces what comes next without adding information, delete it. The gap statement formula — "However, X. Therefore, we need Y." — is simultaneously the close of the current subsection and the implicit opening of the next.

---

### Technical Concept Definition Patterns

New terms, metrics, and equations require consistent definition rituals. The following patterns are mandatory.

---

#### Term Introduction

**RULE**: Introduce a new technical term with italics at first use. Use the format: *Full Name (ACRONYM)*. Always expansion first, acronym in parentheses second. Never reversed.

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "*Memory Level Parallelism (MLP)* can be used to measure miss concurrency."

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "In this work, we first introduce and formally define the concept of *Pure Miss Contribution (PMC)*."

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "At its core, Zion uses *Independent Temporal-Spatial Modules (ITSM)* for broad pattern coverage."

**APPLY**: The moment you write an acronym without this pattern, a confused reviewer is guaranteed. Follow this pattern without exception for every new term in the paper.

---

#### Metric Introduction

**RULE**: Introduce a metric in three parts: (1) what phenomenon it measures, (2) formal definition or equation, (3) interpretation of high vs. low values.

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "Similar to miss rate, the Pure Miss Rate (pMR) can measure the cache efficiency by considering data access concurrency. The formal definition of pMR is as below: [equation]. Based on the C-AMAT model, the memory active cycles on a memory layer are the cycles with memory activities."

**APPLY**: The before-equation sentence must be natural language stating what the equation measures. The after-equation sentence must say what a high or low value means for performance. Equations without both flanking sentences are incomplete.

---

#### Before-Equation Sentence Pattern

**RULE**: Write one natural-language sentence before every equation stating what the equation measures. Write one sentence after every equation interpreting the high/low value.

**Before pattern**: "[Metric] can measure [phenomenon] by considering [factor]."
**After pattern**: "A higher [metric] means [implication]. A lower [metric] means [implication]."

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: Before: "the Pure Miss Rate (pMR) can measure the cache efficiency by considering data access concurrency." After: "[Implied by context] — isolated misses are considered to hurt performance more than concurrent misses."

**APPLY**: Never paste an equation without its natural-language frame. The equation is for precision; the flanking sentences are for comprehension. Both are required.

---

#### C-AMAT Model Introduction Pattern (CARE)

CARE's introduction of the C-AMAT model is a reference example of how to introduce a complex multi-part model.

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "To capture all types of concurrent memory accesses and quantify their impact on performance, a concurrent memory access model named C-AMAT was proposed [44]. In the C-AMAT performance model, a cache access latency is composed of two parts: 1) base access cycles, which are the minimum time an access (hit or miss) needs to spend on a specific cache level; 2) miss access cycles, which are the additional time spent waiting for data in the next levels of the memory hierarchy."

**Pattern**: Named model [citation] → numbered decomposition of components → each component defined → figure reference → interpretation sentence.

**APPLY**: For multi-component models, use a numbered list (1), 2)) for the decomposition. This signals to the reader that the model is complete and exhaustive.

---

### Common Failure Modes

These are patterns that appear regularly in first drafts by PhD students and are consistently flagged by reviewers as signs of weak writing.

---

#### Failure 1: Starting a Subsection with the Technique Name

**WRONG**: "SRRIP is a cache replacement policy that statically predicts..."
**RIGHT**: "Locality-based cache management schemes are designed to increase performance by reducing the total number of misses. SRRIP [19] statically predicts..."

**WHY IT FAILS**: Starting with the technique name forces the reader to hold an unknown referent while absorbing its description. Starting with the phenomenon anchors the reader in something they already understand.

---

#### Failure 2: Passive Voice for Mechanism Description

**WRONG**: "A large unified hardware table is utilized to accommodate multiple prediction streams."
**RIGHT**: "Bingo utilizes a large (114KB) unified hardware table to accommodate and manage multiple prediction streams."

**WHY IT FAILS**: Passive voice obscures agency. The reader cannot tell whether the table is an architecture decision (something you can critique) or a consequence of the problem (something you must accept). Active voice makes the design decision visible.

---

#### Failure 3: Flaw Without Mechanism

**WRONG**: "However, this approach has poor performance on memory-intensive workloads."
**RIGHT**: "However, its count-based confidence mechanism inherently underestimates entries."

**WHY IT FAILS**: "Poor performance" is a result, not a cause. The flaw sentence must name the mechanism that produces the poor performance. A result-only flaw sentence gives the reviewer no way to verify the claim.

---

#### Failure 4: Citation at End of Sentence Instead of After Name

**WRONG**: "IPCP classifies instruction pointers into categories enabling tailored prefetching [7]."
**RIGHT**: "IPCP [7] classifies instruction pointers into categories."

**WHY IT FAILS**: End-of-sentence citations are ambiguous — they could be supporting the entire sentence, a specific claim within it, or the methodology behind it. Citation after the name is unambiguous: it identifies the specific system being described.

---

#### Failure 5: Chronological Organization of Related Work

**WRONG**: Subsection headings of "Early approaches (1990-2005)", "Recent approaches (2010-2020)", "State-of-the-art (2020-present)".
**RIGHT**: Subsection headings of "Re-reference prediction", "Machine learning for cache management", "Cost-based cache management".

**WHY IT FAILS**: Chronological organization implies that newer is always better, which is neither true nor interesting. Functional organization lets you argue that your work occupies a distinct position regardless of when prior systems were published.

---

#### Failure 6: Missing the Gap Statement at Subsection End

**WRONG**: "...resulting in moderate storage overhead. [End of subsection.]"
**RIGHT**: "...resulting in moderate storage overhead. However, its count-based confidence mechanism ignores rare but accurate stream patterns in favor of frequent deltas reducing overall effectiveness."

**WHY IT FAILS**: A subsection that ends on the description of prior art leaves the reader with no direction. Every subsection must close by pointing toward the gap your design fills.

---

#### Failure 7: Grouping Two Systems in One Paragraph

**WRONG**: "SRRIP and DRRIP are both re-reference prediction policies. SRRIP statically predicts... DRRIP builds on SRRIP by..."
**RIGHT**: Separate paragraphs for SRRIP and DRRIP, or handle SRRIP in one sentence using the genealogy formula ("Following in the footsteps of SRRIP, DRRIP...").

**WHY IT FAILS**: Two systems in one paragraph forces the reader to track two simultaneous descriptions. When the paragraph ends, they cannot clearly attribute architecture details or flaws to the correct system. Exception: a minor system that is purely a variation of a major system can be introduced in a single sentence using the genealogy formula.

---

#### Failure 8: The Non-Claim Observation Callout

**WRONG**: "Observation 1. Self-attention workloads have interesting memory behaviors."
**RIGHT**: "Observation 1. Self-attention in LLMs exhibits very high MPKI rates across all levels of the cache hierarchy."

**WHY IT FAILS**: An observation callout must state a falsifiable empirical claim. "Interesting memory behaviors" is not falsifiable. The callout sentence is the headline; it must contain the finding, not describe that a finding exists.

---

#### Failure 9: Using "Proposes" or "Achieves" as a Mechanism Verb

**WRONG**: "IPCP [7] proposes a classification of instruction pointers..."
**RIGHT**: "IPCP [7] classifies instruction pointers into categories..."

**WHY IT FAILS**: "Proposes" is a verb about the paper, not the system. You are describing how the system works, not what the paper did. The distinction matters: "proposes" is author-centric (it describes a rhetorical act), while "classifies" is system-centric (it describes a hardware operation). In systems architecture, describe the hardware, not the authors.

---

#### Failure 10: Omitting the Quantitative Boundary in Background

**WRONG**: "In all benchmarks, a significant portion of misses have hit-miss overlapping."
**RIGHT**: "In all benchmarks, 30% to 80% misses have hit-miss overlapping."

**WHY IT FAILS**: "Significant portion" is unfalsifiable. The specific range (30%–80%) tells the reader how large the effect is, makes the claim verifiable, and anchors the motivation for the design. If you write "significant" or "substantial" or "notable" without a number, you have not done the measurement.

---

*End of Chapter 2.*

## Chapter 3: Design & Methodology

*A permanent style reference for systems and architecture PhD students, synthesized from forensic analysis of two accepted papers: Zion (MICRO) and CARE (TPDS). Every rule is grounded in direct quotation from those papers.*

---

### Design Section Title and Opening

---

**RULE**: The section title encodes the system name plus a multi-adjective claim about what the system is — not a generic label like "System Design" or "Our Approach." The title asserts before the reader reads a word of body text.

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: The section heading reads "III. ZION: A COMPREHENSIVE AND ADAPTIVE PREFETCHER" — the system name appears first, then two adjectives that match the paper's central contribution claim.

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: The section heading reads "V. CARE: CONCURRENCY-AWARE CACHE MANAGEMENT" — the name leads, the adjective phrase frames the design's differentiating property.

**APPLY**: Your section title is a claim, not a label. If you cannot fit the system's core adjective into the title, your contribution framing is not yet sharp enough.

---

**RULE**: The first paragraph of the design section follows a strict three-sentence architecture. Sentence 1: propose/introduce the system with its adjective chain and goal. Sentence 2: at its core, the primary mechanism. Sentence 3: also, the secondary or complementary mechanism. Zero roadmap sentences ("in this section we describe...") are permitted anywhere in this paragraph.

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "We propose Zion, a comprehensive, adaptive and lightweight prefetcher designed to accelerate memory-intensive workloads. At the core, it features ITSM, an independent temporal-spatial modules design that provides predictions by achieving comprehensive coverage of both temporal and spatial locality accesses. Additionally, Zion employs runtime feedback to dynamically adjust prefetch decisions and insertion destinations, optimizing performance while minimizing memory pressure."

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "We introduce CARE, an LLC management framework that considers both data locality and data concurrency. The primary goal of CARE is to improve locality while utilizing concurrency to reduce the overall performance penalty of cache misses. CARE learns the re-reference characteristics and PMC values of the cache blocks by associating each cache reference with a PC-based signature."

**APPLY**: Write these three sentences before any subsection. If you find yourself writing a fourth sentence that begins with "In Section X, we describe...", delete it. The subsection headers do that work.

---

**RULE**: When a subsection addresses a failure mode of the mechanism introduced just above it, open with the concessive-consequence-mitigation pattern: "Although [X provides Y], unmanaged [X] causes [Z]. To address this, [mitigation]." This signals to reviewers that the design is self-aware.

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "Although Zion provides both temporal and spatial pattern recognition, unmanaged and overly aggressive predictions may cause severe cache pollution, increased memory pressure, and degraded performance. To address this, Zion employs three adjustment strategies to dynamically manage the behavior of ITSM at the L2 cache level."

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: The DTRM subsection opens with the same logic: CARE introduces dynamic threshold reconfiguration because fixed thresholds cannot adapt across workload phases — the limitation of the prior PMCS quantization scheme is stated before the solution is named.

**APPLY**: Use this pattern exactly once per major failure mode. Overuse flattens the rhetorical effect. If you are addressing a second failure mode, vary the opener.

---

### Subsection Blueprint

---

**RULE**: The first sentence of every subsection is a claim or a component description — never a meta-statement about what the subsection will do. The subsection heading already announces the topic; the first sentence advances it.

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: The subsection "A. ITSM: Independent Temporal-Spatial Modules" opens: "Independent Temporal-Spatial Modules (ITSM) consist of multiple prediction modules that leverage IP to predict memory access patterns by tracking both deltas and strides." This is a component description that immediately tells the reader what ITSM is, not what the subsection will explain.

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: The subsection "A. Overview" opens: "Figure 6 shows a high-level overview of CARE." — an immediate structural anchor, not a promise about what follows.

**APPLY**: If your first sentence contains "this subsection," "we describe," "we present," or "this section explains," replace it with a sentence that makes a factual claim about the mechanism you are introducing.

---

**RULE**: Named sub-components within a subsection use bold inline labels followed immediately by their functional role or triggering condition. The label is a noun phrase; the first sentence after the label is the mechanism's first action, not a warmup sentence.

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "**Predictions.** Figure 3 presents a high-level overview of ITSM. To make prefetching decisions, the number of candidates issued by Zion is limited to those targeting addresses within the same physical page..." — the bold label is followed immediately by a figure reference that anchors the architectural discussion.

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "**Training.** Besides making prediction decisions, ITSM also leverages every demand access to dynamically update the MTs, ensuring that they remain relevant to changing memory access patterns." — the first word after the label is the mechanism's primary action ("leverages every demand access").

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "**Range-Segmented Monitoring-Tables.** To ensure efficient hardware realization for predictions, ITSM adopts a cache-like structure for each segmented MT." — the label announces the component; the sentence announces the design rationale simultaneously.

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "**Metadata bits in cache blocks.** The single R bit is used to track the re-reference behavior of each cache block." — one sentence, one field, one function. No warmup.

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "**Update SHT on hit accesses.** If the cache block receives a hit, the R bit of the block is set to 1." — trigger condition in the first clause, system action in the second clause, within one sentence.

**APPLY**: Write the bold label, then immediately write what the component does or what triggers it. Anything else is throat-clearing. All four information elements — name, trigger, action, consequence — should appear within two sentences maximum.

---

**RULE**: Sub-component labels are always noun phrases, never sentences. They name the component or operation, not describe it.

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "Predictions." / "Training." / "Range-Segmented Monitoring-Tables." / "Accuracy Monitoring." / "Real-time System Feedback." — all are noun phrases.

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "Metadata bits in cache blocks." / "SHT entry structures." / "Update SHT on hit accesses." / "Update SHT on eviction." / "Insertion policy." / "Hit-promotion policy." / "Victim selection." / "Writeback-aware." — all are noun phrases or gerund phrases, never complete sentences.

**APPLY**: If you are tempted to write a bold label like "**How CARE Handles Cache Eviction.**" — shorten it to "**Cache Eviction.**" or "**Victim Selection.**" The body sentence does the explaining.

---

### Hardware Mechanism Description Grammar

---

**RULE (Active/Passive)**: Use active voice when the system or a named component is the grammatical subject performing an action. Use passive voice only when a data field, bit, or counter is the topic and the system action is incidental. Never use passive for the main system's design actions.

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: Active: "Zion continuously monitors inactive MTs, and if a new, in-progress, period monitoring reaches the required threshold, MT is reactivated." — Zion is the actor. The MT reactivation, a consequent state, uses passive appropriately.

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: Active: "ITSM distributes the prediction process across these five distinct MTs, each predicting the most likely next delta or stride for a given IP to generate prediction addresses within the Zion prefetcher." — ITSM acts; generation is a consequence.

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: Active: "CARE assigns/updates the EPV counter for each block based on the prediction information from SBP." Active: "CARE selects a victim block whose EPV is 3." Active: "SBP identifies the re-reference behavior of cache blocks as High-Reuse if the associated RC counter is saturated at its maximum value."

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: Appropriate passive for field state: "the R bit of the block is set to 1" — the R bit is the topic, the action is a consequence of a hit event.

**APPLY**: Before submitting, grep your design section for "is performed by," "is done by," "is used to," "is applied by." Each hit is a candidate for active-voice revision. Reserve passive for state-of-fields paragraphs.

---

**RULE**: Never write that a system "tries to," "aims to," "attempts to," "may perform," or "is intended to." These hedges signal that you do not yet understand your own mechanism. Use declarative verbs: classifies, allocates, directs, monitors, decrements, assigns, records, discards, redirects, increments, quantizes.

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: The paper never hedges the system's actions. Zion "monitors," "redirects," "employs," "evaluates," "encodes," "computes," "discards," "reactivates."

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: CARE "learns," "augments," "assigns," "updates," "selects," "inserts," "decrements," "quantizes," "increments," "distinguishes." Not once does it "try to learn" or "aim to assign."

**APPLY**: If your draft contains "aims to," read the sentence aloud and replace with the actual verb. "CARE aims to reduce eviction priority" becomes "CARE decrements the eviction priority."

---

**RULE (Condition-Action)**: State the triggering condition before the system's action. Use the pattern "When [event], [system] [action]." For binary conditions, use parallel flat structure: "If [condition], [consequence]. If [condition], [consequence]. Therefore, [invariant]." For the negative case, use "Otherwise, [action]." Do not invert these.

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "When 25% or 12.5% of MSHR is available, an MT must have the second or third level, respectively, to remain active." — condition first, consequence second.

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "If a matching entry is found in the MT, the confidence score of the corresponding entry is incremented. Otherwise, a new entry is inserted into the corresponding MT." — flat binary, condition-then-consequence for each branch.

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "On a mismatch, the stored stride is updated; otherwise, the confidence score is incremented for the matching entry." — temporal trigger ("On a mismatch") with immediate consequence, binary otherwise.

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "If the PMC value of a miss is smaller than PMC_low, the PMCS of the corresponding cache block is 0. If the PMC value of a miss is larger than PMC_high, the PMCS of the corresponding cache block is set to 3. If the value of PMC is between the two thresholds, the corresponding PMCS is 1." — three parallel flat conditionals, each a single sentence.

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "When a miss access allocates an MSHR entry, the PMC counter associated with the miss will be initialized to zero." — condition clause leads.

**APPLY**: If you catch yourself writing "The system performs action X when condition Y occurs," reverse the clause order: "When Y occurs, the system performs X."

---

**RULE (Saturation)**: When a counter or threshold operates in a saturating manner, state this parenthetically in the same sentence as the update operation — never in a separate sentence.

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "CARE increments the RC counter (in a saturated manner), which corresponds to the signature of the block in the SHT." — parenthetical, inline.

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "the related PD counter in the SHT is decremented (in a saturated manner)" — parenthetical, inline.

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "PMC low and PMC high are decreased by 10 and 70 cycles, respectively" — no saturation qualifier needed here because the DTRM section had already established that these are bounded thresholds; the parenthetical is reserved for counter operations.

**APPLY**: Write "(in a saturated manner)" as a parenthetical immediately after the counter verb. Do not write "This operation is saturated at [value]" as a standalone sentence.

---

**RULE (Counter Semantics)**: When introducing a counter, state the meaning of its zero and saturated states explicitly. Use the pattern: "A [zero/positive/saturated] [counter] [indicates/implies] [consequence]."

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "A zero RC value indicates the future blocks associated with this signature are rarely reused. A positive RC counter implies that the future blocks associated with this signature have data locality, and they are likely to receive cache hits."

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "An EPV of zero implies that a cache block has the lowest eviction priority. An EPV of saturation implies that a cache block has a high eviction priority and will be evicted sooner."

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "Similarly, a larger PD value means that the cache misses associated with that signature have a high probability of having large PMC values in the future."

**APPLY**: Every counter you introduce should have its boundary semantics stated in two sentences: one for zero, one for saturation (or maximum). Reviewers use these sentences to verify that your counter logic is consistent with your eviction and insertion policies.

---

**RULE (Sequence of Operations)**: Describe multi-step hardware processes using temporal connectives — "begins by," "then," "simultaneously," "before," "on a mismatch" — not numbered prose lists. Reserve numbered lists for parameter tables or bullet-point hardware specs.

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "The training phase begins by processing each memory address to compute the delta. This computed delta, based on the range, determines which MT should be updated. Similar to the prediction phase, ITSM uses the IP to index the appropriate MT and update it. If a matching entry is found in the MT, the confidence score of the corresponding entry is incremented. Otherwise, a new entry is inserted into the corresponding MT."

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "Simultaneously, the fifth MT targets spatial locality by predicting strides. During the training process, currently accessed IP is compressed to index the corresponding table. Before updating the stored prior block of an IP to the current block, using these two subsequently accessed blocks, the current stride is computed and compared with the prior stride stored. On a mismatch, the stored stride is updated."

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "For every new cache access, CARE first extracts the signature from the PC of the cache request. The signature of the access is used to index into the SHT. Each SHT entry tracks the cache accesses associated with a specific PC-based signature."

**APPLY**: If you have written "Step 1: ..., Step 2: ..., Step 3: ..." in a prose paragraph, replace the step numbers with temporal connectives. Numbered steps belong in algorithm pseudocode, not in mechanism description paragraphs.

---

**RULE (Parenthetical Figure Callouts)**: When describing a sequence of operations that corresponds to labeled elements in a nearby figure, embed the figure label as a parenthetical at the end of the relevant clause. Use uppercase letters in parentheses: "( A )", "( B )", etc. Do not interrupt the sentence structure.

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "The workload begins by processing each demand request and extracting the IP associated with the corresponding memory access ( A ). We then compress the IP using a hash function and simultaneously index the five MTs ( B ). Four MTs use deltas to capture temporal locality, while a single MT targets strides to capture spatial locality, enabling comprehensive pattern recognition. ITSM distributes the prediction process across these five distinct MTs ( C ), each predicting the most likely next delta or stride for a given IP to generate prediction addresses within the Zion prefetcher ( D )."

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "For every MT prediction ( 1 ), a confidence-based filtering mechanism applies empirically selected thresholds... ( 2 )."

**APPLY**: Parenthetical figure callouts are a compact way to tie prose to diagram labels. Use them to walk reviewers through the figure without duplicating the caption. One callout per clause is the maximum.

---

**RULE (Alternative-Then-Decision)**: When you chose your design over a discarded alternative, state the alternative, name its cost, conclude with your choice, and justify in three sentences or fewer.

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "Another solution is to consider the recency information and evict the least recent block [in victim selection]. Through testing, there is no discernible performance difference between the two solutions. However, recording the recency information for each cache block requires a huge hardware cost. Therefore, CARE selects the candidate randomly." (CARE paper)

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "Unlike LRU, which inserts all cache blocks to the MRU position of the 'LRU chain', CARE dynamically learns the re-reference and PMC information of each specific signature." — the alternative (LRU's fixed insertion) is named in the opening clause; the design choice follows immediately.

**APPLY**: The alternative-then-decision pattern signals design maturity. Use it at least once per major policy choice (insertion policy, replacement policy, threshold). Do not use more than three sentences for the justification, or you are defending rather than explaining.

### Figure Reference Rules

---

**RULE**: When a figure is the primary evidence for a paragraph's claim, name it as the grammatical subject of the paragraph's opening sentence. Do not bury it parenthetically at the sentence's end.

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "Figure 3 presents a high-level overview of ITSM." — the figure is the subject; "presents" is the active verb.

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "Figure 2(a) presents a region illustrating dense clusters of accesses with high spatial locality and small, varying strides." — figure as subject, verb describes what it shows structurally.

**EVIDENCE (ZION)**: "Figure 4: Zion architecture in the memory hierarchy." — the figure caption for the architecture diagram is a short noun phrase only.

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "Figure 6 shows a high-level overview of CARE." — first sentence of the Overview subsection; the figure anchors the entire subsection.

**APPLY**: If your paragraph's first sentence is a claim about the mechanism and you cite the figure parenthetically at the end ("...as shown in Figure X"), consider whether the figure should lead. For architecture overview paragraphs, the figure almost always leads.

---

**RULE**: The interpretive claim — what the figure means for your argument — goes in the second sentence, not in the figure-introduction sentence. The figure introduction sentence is structural; the second sentence is argumentative.

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "Figure 2(a) presents a region illustrating dense clusters of accesses with high spatial locality and small, varying strides. These behaviors are typically associated with token-level operations such as embedding lookups." — first sentence: structural description; second sentence: semantic interpretation.

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "Figure 2(b) provides a zoomed-out view of the accesses shown in Figure 2(a), highlighting a phase spanning tens of thousands of addresses. Figure 2(b) exhibits phases similar to those in mcf, characterized by highly fragmented and irregular memory access patterns, which pose significant challenges for traditional prefetching strategies." — first sentence: structural description; second sentence: argumentative interpretation.

**APPLY**: Do not write "Figure 6 shows that CARE outperforms LRU because..." in a single sentence. Split it: "Figure 6 shows [structure]. [Claim about what this means for the argument]."

---

**RULE**: Mid-paragraph figure references (not paragraph-opening references) use "as shown in Figure N" at the end of the clause, not as a parenthetical "(Figure N)" at the sentence's end.

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "three adjustment strategies to dynamically manage the behavior of ITSM at the L2 cache level, as shown in Figure 4."

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: The paper uses this pattern consistently for supporting evidence mid-paragraph.

**APPLY**: Reserve "(Figure N)" at sentence end for tables. For inline figure cross-references, use "as shown in Figure N" placed at the end of the clause it supports.

---

**RULE**: Figure captions for hardware block diagrams are short noun phrases only, with no active verbs. Figure captions for data figures add an axis-encoding parenthetical.

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "Fig. 4: Zion architecture in the memory hierarchy." — noun phrase, no verb.

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "Fig. 2: Various access patterns in self-attention workloads exhibiting temporal evolution: (a) locally-regular pattern and (b) globally-irregular and unpredictable patterns." — noun phrase with embedded structure description.

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "Fig. 6: Block diagram of CARE." — four words, noun phrase.

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "Fig. 5: Distribution of PMC. (The x-axis represents the value of PMC in cycles. 1: 0-49 cycles; 2: 50-99 cycles; ...)" — data figure caption adds axis encoding in parenthetical.

**APPLY**: Write your caption as a noun phrase first. If the figure is a data plot, add "(x-axis: [what]; y-axis: [what])" as a parenthetical. If the figure is a block diagram or architecture schematic, the noun phrase alone is sufficient.

### Algorithm and Control Logic Presentation

---

**RULE**: Formal algorithms require a pseudocode block. The block header states (a) what the algorithm measures or computes, and (b) its invocation trigger. Variable declarations with one-line descriptions appear at the top of the block before the first procedural line.

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: Algorithm 1 is titled "Measure PMC for cache misses at cache level l (called every active memory cycle)" — the title states the computation ("Measure PMC") and the trigger ("called every active memory cycle"). The block opens with three variable declarations before any procedural code: "NoNewAccess_x: single-bit cycle status identifier per core; set if no overlapping opportunities in this cycle" / "PMC: field in MSHR to calculate the pure miss contribution for a miss block; initialized to 0" / "update(N_x): check the number of outstanding misses from core x at cache level l in this cycle."

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: Zion does not present a formal pseudocode algorithm — its control logic is conveyed entirely through annotated figures and temporal-connective prose, demonstrating that pseudocode is appropriate only when the logic has conditional branching that cannot be cleanly linearized in prose.

**APPLY**: Use pseudocode when your mechanism has three or more conditional branches that would require nested prose conditionals to express. If the mechanism is a pipeline of sequential steps, prose with temporal connectives (as in Zion) is preferred over pseudocode.

---

**RULE**: For bulleted hardware parameter lists, each bullet must contain four elements: label, range or value, size or width, and rationale. All four must be present on every bullet.

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "MT1: [−8,0) ∪ (0,+8] with 512 entries, covering the smallest and most frequent deltas, which typically indicate accesses with high spatial locality." — label (MT1), range ([−8,0) ∪ (0,+8]), size (512 entries), rationale (smallest and most frequent deltas, high spatial locality).

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "MT2: [−16,−8] ∪ [+8,+16] with 320 entries, handling slightly larger deltas while maintaining separation from the smaller range." — label (MT2), range, size (320 entries), rationale (slightly larger deltas, separation maintained).

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "MT4: [−63,−32] ∪ [+32,+63] with 64 entries, targeting the largest and least frequent deltas." — label (MT4), range, size (64 entries), rationale (largest and least frequent).

**APPLY**: If you find yourself writing a bullet that has only a label and a value without rationale, you have not finished the sentence. The rationale is what connects the parameter to your design argument.

---

**RULE**: Empirically selected thresholds must be flagged as empirically selected, or must be accompanied by a "Based on our analysis" sentence that gives the reasoning for the specific value chosen. Do not present empirical values without attribution.

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "a confidence-based filtering mechanism applies empirically selected thresholds of 20%, 25%, 30%, and 40% for delta-based MT1 to MT4, and 1% is set for stride-based MT5."

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "Based on our analysis, smaller deltas occur more frequently and exhibit shorter reuse distances; therefore, larger tables are allocated to accommodate more entries."

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "We choose the values empirically based on the results of a large number of simulations." — this sentence appears after stating the specific increment values (10 cycles for PMC_low, 70 cycles for PMC_high), linking value choice to empirical process.

**APPLY**: For every constant in your design (thresholds, period lengths, counter widths, table sizes) ask: is the reader given enough information to understand why this value was chosen? If not, add either "empirically selected" or a "Based on our analysis" sentence.

---

**RULE**: Multi-step processes in prose use temporal connectives. Specifically: "begins by" for the first step, "then" for sequential steps, "simultaneously" for parallel operations, "before" for operations that must precede another, "on a mismatch" or "otherwise" for conditional branches.

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "The training phase begins by processing each memory address to compute the delta. This computed delta, based on the range, determines which MT should be updated... If a matching entry is found in the MT, the confidence score of the corresponding entry is incremented. Otherwise, a new entry is inserted into the corresponding MT. The training phase ensures that the prediction model continuously adapts to evolving memory access patterns."

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "Simultaneously, the fifth MT targets spatial locality by predicting strides. During the training process, currently accessed IP is compressed to index the corresponding table. Before updating the stored prior block of an IP to the current block, using these two subsequently accessed blocks, the current stride is computed and compared with the prior stride stored. On a mismatch, the stored stride is updated."

**APPLY**: Each temporal connective carries information about ordering or concurrency. "Simultaneously" tells the reviewer that the two operations share the same hardware cycle. "Before" tells the reviewer there is a dependency. Do not use these words interchangeably.

### Methodology Section Blueprint

---

**RULE**: The methodology section begins immediately with the first bold-label topic sentence. There is no introductory paragraph, no "In this section, we describe our experimental methodology," and no transitional preamble. The first word of the section is a bold label.

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: The section "IV. METHODOLOGY" opens with the bold label "**Simulation Setup.**" — the very first sentence of the section is "We evaluate the performance of Zion using the ChampSim simulator [26], which has been employed in the 2nd and 3rd Data Prefetching Championships [27], [28]." No introductory paragraph precedes it.

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: The section "VI. METHODOLOGY" opens with the bold label "**Simulated system.**" — the very first sentence is "We evaluate CARE against prior cache management schemes using the version of ChampSim [4] used for the 1st instruction prefetching competition (IPC-1 [1])." Direct entry, no warmup.

**APPLY**: Delete your methodology section's first paragraph if it begins with anything other than a bold label. The section heading already announces the topic.

---

**RULE (Simulator Paragraph)**: State the simulator name and citation, the competition in which it was used and that competition's citation, the default hardware configuration name, and a pointer to the parameter table. These four elements appear in this order, within two sentences.

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "We evaluate the performance of Zion using the ChampSim simulator [26], which has been employed in the 2nd and 3rd Data Prefetching Championships [27], [28]. We simulate the Intel Alder Lake (GLC) multi-core processor as the default microarchitecture, supporting configurations ranging from 1-core to 8-core systems. Table 2(A) outlines the system parameters."

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "We evaluate CARE against prior cache management schemes using the version of ChampSim [4] used for the 1st instruction prefetching competition (IPC-1 [1]). The details of the configuration parameters are described in Table VII."

**APPLY**: The simulator paragraph is a credentialing paragraph — it establishes that your simulation infrastructure is community-standard. Cite both the simulator and the championship in which it was used. Reviewers from DPC/CRC know these benchmarks and will check the citation.

---

**RULE (Second Hardware Configuration)**: When a second hardware configuration is evaluated, introduce it in one sentence with the pattern "To explore [broader goal], we also simulate [system X], representing [category]." Then point to its parameter table entry.

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "To explore architectural diversity, we also simulate the Apple A14 Firestorm processor, representing modern mobile and edge platforms, in a single-core configuration. Its detailed parameters are listed in Table 2(B)."

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "For multi-core configurations, we scale the size of the LLC in proportion to the number of cores." — a different form of the second-configuration rule for multi-core scaling rather than a second processor.

**APPLY**: The "To explore [X]" framing gives the second configuration a purpose rather than making it look like padding. Never introduce a second configuration without stating what question it is designed to answer.

---

**RULE (Workload Selection)**: State the numerical filter criterion for workload inclusion. Name the metric and the threshold value. Never write "memory-intensive workloads" without quantifying what memory-intensive means.

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "We select 37 traces, each exhibiting LLC miss per kilo instructions (MPKI) greater than 1 in the baseline system without prefetching."

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "which have at least 1 LLC miss per kilo instructions (MPKI) in the single-core baseline system with no prefetching." Table VIII shows LLC MPKI for every evaluated workload.

**APPLY**: The threshold sentence makes your workload selection reproducible. A reviewer should be able to apply your filter to any benchmark suite and arrive at the same set. "We selected challenging workloads" is not reproducible.

---

**RULE (Warm-Up)**: State warm-up length and measurement length in one sentence. Both numbers must appear; neither can be omitted.

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "For every benchmark cache is initialized through warm-up of 50M sim-point instructions and statistics are kept for the next 200M sim-point instructions."

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: (for LLM traces) "For all simulations, the first 20% of instructions are used for warm-up, and the remaining 80% are used for evaluation."

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "we warm up each core using 50M instructions from each workload, and then run simulation over the next 200M instructions."

**APPLY**: The warm-up sentence is two numbers: the warm-up count and the measurement count. If your warm-up is percentage-based, state the percentage and note what the absolute count is for your representative trace. Omitting either number invites reviewer questions about steady-state validity.

---

**RULE (Metrics)**: Define metrics inline at first use with the pattern "X, defined as the ratio of A to B." Do not create a standalone Metrics paragraph unless the section structure explicitly requires it.

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "We evaluate prefetch coverage, defined as the ratio of cache misses eliminated by the prefetcher to the number of misses in the baseline (no prefetching)." — inline definition at first use, within the results section rather than methodology.

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "we report the weighted speedup over LRU, which is commonly used to evaluate shared caches [2], [41]." — metric named, community citation provided, inline.

**APPLY**: Metrics definitions should appear where the metric is first introduced. If you introduce a metric in the methodology and use it again in results, the definition belongs in methodology. If a metric appears only in results, define it there.

---

**RULE (Ablation Naming)**: Define the ablation variant using the pattern: "The only difference from [SYSTEM] is that [ABLATION] does not consider [variable] but uses [alternative]." This makes the ablation's purpose unambiguous.

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "The only difference from CARE is that M-CARE does not consider PMC but uses MLP-based cost to analyze data access concurrency and guide cache management." — M-CARE is defined entirely by what it removes from CARE and what it substitutes.

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: Zion's ablation is handled structurally through the five-MT configuration and the dynamic disabling mechanism; individual MT contributions are isolated by the period-based effectiveness monitoring.

**APPLY**: Name your ablation variant, then write exactly one sentence using this template. The sentence should make clear which variable is isolated. If it takes more than one sentence to define the ablation, you are either defining multiple ablations or your baseline design is not well-specified.

---

**RULE (Compared Schemes)**: List all comparison systems in one sentence with citations. Maintain the same ordering of comparison systems throughout the paper — in the methodology paragraph, in every result table, and in every result figure.

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "We compare Zion against four SOTA hardware prefetchers: IPCP [7], Bingo [9], SPP [6], and Berti [8]." — one sentence, four systems, four citations. This exact order (IPCP, Bingo, SPP, Berti) is maintained in all result tables and figures.

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "We compare CARE against three state-of-the-art LLC management schemes: SHiP++ [53], Hawkeye [17], Glider [42], and Mockingjay [41]." — one sentence, with the same ordering maintained in Figure 7, Figure 8, Figure 9, Figure 10, and Figure 11.

**APPLY**: Fix the ordering of your comparison systems in the methodology paragraph, then audit every figure and table to confirm the ordering is identical. Inconsistent ordering is a common source of reviewer confusion and can make results tables difficult to compare across subsections.

### Hardware Overhead Reporting

---

**RULE**: Report hardware overhead in two numbers: absolute size (KB) and percentage of the target structure's capacity. Both numbers appear in the same sentence.

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "the hardware overhead of CARE is around 26.64KB, which is only 1.3% of the capacity of a 2MB LLC." — absolute (26.64KB) and relative (1.3% of 2MB LLC) in one sentence.

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "CARE only needs 6.76KB to support concurrency awareness." — absolute number for the incremental concurrency-aware cost, setting up the comparison with the total.

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "Overall, Zion is lightweight, requiring only 9.03KB of storage for the 1-core configuration. This achieves 12.6× and 2.2× lower storage overhead than SOTA prefetchers Bingo [9] and Berti [8]." — absolute number, then comparative multipliers against named competitors.

**APPLY**: Never report only an absolute KB number without context. The percentage-of-structure or multiplier-vs-competitor number is what gives the reviewer a sense of whether the overhead is acceptable. Both forms are acceptable; use whichever gives the stronger argument.

---

**RULE**: When overhead scales with configuration (e.g., number of cores, LLC capacity), state the scaling law in one sentence.

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "This cost scales linearly with the LLC capacity." — one sentence, linear scaling law.

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: Table 1 provides a per-structure breakdown of storage cost, implicitly showing that the overhead is constant per core configuration rather than superlinear.

**APPLY**: If your overhead doubles with a doubling of cores, state this. If it scales sublinearly, state this and give the formula. Reviewers will ask; answer it in the paper.

---

**RULE**: When reporting overhead in a table, include all structural components as rows, with fields broken down by bit width and entry count. The total line appears at the bottom of each structural group and again at the paper-wide total.

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: Table 1 breaks ITSM into five MT rows with format "Field (#bits in each entry) | Entries | #Bits," then groups ITSM total (8.4KB), Predictions (subtotal), Period (subtotal), Metadata (subtotal), Tracker total (0.64KB), and Zion Total (9.03KB). Every row shows the decomposition.

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: Table V shows every structural component (NoNewAccess bit, lookup table, PMC register, PMC_low, PMC_high, TCM, EPV bits, prefetch bit, signature bits, R bit, PMCS bits, RC counter, PD counter) with its size and functional use. The total is 26.64KB, with the concurrency-aware increment highlighted as 6.76KB.

**APPLY**: Your overhead table is the primary artifact reviewers use to verify your claimed storage cost. Every bit must be accounted for. Do not round up silently; show the calculation.

### Common Failure Modes

---

**FAILURE MODE 1: The roadmap paragraph.**

Students write the design section's opening paragraph as a roadmap: "In this section, we describe the design of [SYSTEM]. First, we present [component A]. Then, we describe [component B]. Finally, we explain [component C]." This wastes the opening and delays the claim by one full paragraph.

The correct form: three sentences — propose, core mechanism, secondary mechanism. The subsection headers do the roadmap work.

---

**FAILURE MODE 2: Meta-commentary as subsection opener.**

"In this subsection, we explain how CARE handles cache evictions." This sentence tells the reader nothing that the subsection heading did not already tell them. The first sentence of a subsection should advance the argument — it should make a claim about the mechanism or state the mechanism's function.

Correct form: "CARE selects a victim block whose EPV is 3." One sentence. Immediate mechanism.

---

**FAILURE MODE 3: Hedged system verbs.**

"CARE tries to reduce the number of pure misses." "Zion aims to minimize cache pollution." "The mechanism may improve performance." Systems do not try, aim, or may. They classify, allocate, redirect, discard, and monitor. Hedged verbs signal that the student does not have confidence in their own mechanism's behavior. If the behavior is truly probabilistic, describe the mechanism that produces the probability — do not hedge the verb.

---

**FAILURE MODE 4: Passive voice for system actions.**

"The prefetch decision is made by ITSM." "Cache eviction is performed by the EPV counter." These constructions hide the agent. Rewrite: "ITSM makes the prefetch decision." "The EPV counter governs cache eviction." Passive is appropriate only when the field or bit is the topic, not when the system is the actor.

---

**FAILURE MODE 5: Condition stated after action.**

"The confidence score is incremented when a matching entry is found." This reverses the temporal and logical order of the hardware operation. The hardware evaluates the condition first, then performs the action. Write it that way: "If a matching entry is found, the confidence score is incremented."

---

**FAILURE MODE 6: Numbered prose steps.**

"Step 1: The IP is extracted. Step 2: The IP is compressed using a hash function. Step 3: The compressed IP indexes all five MTs." Numbered steps belong in algorithm pseudocode, not in mechanism description prose. Replace with temporal connectives: "The workload begins by extracting the IP from the demand request. The IP is then compressed using a hash function and simultaneously indexes all five MTs."

---

**FAILURE MODE 7: Missing workload filter quantification.**

"We evaluate on memory-intensive SPEC workloads." This is not reproducible. State the filter: "We select traces exhibiting LLC MPKI greater than 1 in the no-prefetch baseline." The exact threshold, the exact metric, the exact baseline configuration. Reviewers who replicate the evaluation need all three.

---

**FAILURE MODE 8: Methodology introductory paragraph.**

"In this section, we describe the experimental methodology used to evaluate [SYSTEM]. We first describe the simulation setup, then the workloads, and finally the comparison schemes." Delete this. The methodology section begins with the first bold label.

---

**FAILURE MODE 9: Overhead without percentage context.**

"The total hardware overhead of [SYSTEM] is 12KB." This number is meaningless without context. What percentage of the LLC is 12KB? How does it compare to related work? State both: "The total hardware overhead is around 12KB, which is only 0.6% of a 2MB LLC, and 2.3× lower than [PRIOR WORK]."

---

**FAILURE MODE 10: Figure as parenthetical for primary evidence.**

"CARE outperforms all comparison schemes in the 4-core configuration (Figure 7)." When a figure is the primary evidence for a paragraph's claim, it should be the subject of the paragraph's first sentence: "Figure 7 shows the normalized IPC across all 4-core multi-copy workloads." The claim — CARE outperforms — becomes the second sentence.

---

**FAILURE MODE 11: Ablation undefined by difference.**

"We evaluate an ablation variant called M-CARE." The reader does not know what M-CARE removes. Always follow the ablation name with: "The only difference from CARE is that M-CARE does not consider PMC but uses MLP-based cost to analyze data access concurrency." One sentence defines the ablation completely.

---

**FAILURE MODE 12: Counter semantics omitted.**

A 3-bit PD counter is introduced. The reader is never told what a zero value means or what a saturated value means. Then the cache management policy uses PD to make decisions. The reader must reverse-engineer the counter semantics from the policy description. State counter semantics at introduction: "A zero PD value indicates misses associated with this signature are low-cost. A saturated PD value indicates misses are high-cost." Then the policy becomes self-explanatory.

---

**FAILURE MODE 13: Bold labels that are full sentences.**

"**How CARE Handles the Moderate-Reuse Case.**" This is a sentence. Bold inline labels are noun phrases: "**Moderate-Reuse Policy.**" The sentence explaining the case is the body text that follows the label.

---

**FAILURE MODE 14: Comparison system ordering inconsistency.**

The methodology paragraph lists comparison systems as A, B, C, D. Table 1 shows them as A, C, B, D. Figure 5 shows them as B, A, D, C. This forces reviewers to re-map on every page. Fix the ordering once in the methodology paragraph and enforce it globally.

## Chapter 4: Evaluation & Conclusion

*Every rule below is grounded in forensic analysis of two accepted papers: Zion (MICRO 2024, hardware prefetcher) and CARE (TPDS, concurrency-aware cache management). Both papers were written by the same lab. Their evaluation and conclusion sections are treated here as canonical specimens. Each rule names the paper(s) that supply the evidence, quotes the exact passage, and closes with a one-sentence application note. Rules are ordered by the frequency with which PhD students violate them.*

---

## 1. Evaluation Section Opening

### The Core Question

Do you begin the evaluation section with a prose paragraph, or do you dive directly into results?

Both answers are correct for different paper structures. The error is writing a long scoping preamble when the section heading already communicates scope, or writing no orientation when the reader genuinely needs to know what is being shown before the first figure appears.

---

**RULE 1.1**: If results are presented on a single dimension with a single primary metric, skip the scoping paragraph entirely. Begin with the first subsection heading and then immediately with the figure-pointer sentence.

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: Zion's evaluation section (Section V) contains no scoping paragraph at all. The section heading reads "V. EXPERIMENTAL RESULTS" and the first subsection heading reads "A. Performance of SPEC workloads." The very next line is the figure-pointer sentence: "Figure 5(a) presents the normalized speedup of all evaluated prefetchers running 37 homogeneous SPEC workloads under various system configurations."

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: N/A — CARE uses a one-sentence scoping paragraph, which is the contrasting case documented in Rule 1.2.

**APPLY**: If your section heading already names the scope (e.g., "Performance Evaluation"), a preamble that restates scope is redundant — cut it.

---

**RULE 1.2**: If results are multi-dimensional — multiple metrics, multiple system configurations, or multiple workload families — one sentence of scoping is acceptable. The sentence must name the system, the comparison class, the configuration, and the setting. It must not announce what the results show.

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "We show the performance results of CARE over state-of-the-art schemes in 4-core configuration with prefetching in this section." This sentence names: system (CARE), comparison class (state-of-the-art schemes), configuration (4-core), setting (with prefetching). It announces nothing about the outcome. It is exactly one sentence.

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: No equivalent sentence. Zion's evaluation covers five configurations (1-core A14, 1-core GLC, 2-core, 4-core, 8-core) and two workload families (SPEC, self-attention), yet the authors deemed the section headings alone sufficient for orientation.

**APPLY**: If you write a scoping sentence, test it by asking whether it would still be accurate if your system performed worse than baseline — if yes, it is appropriately neutral; if not, you have leaked a result claim into the opening, which is forbidden.

---

**RULE 1.3**: The scoping paragraph must never state or imply results. "We show that CARE substantially outperforms..." is a result leak. "We show the performance results of CARE over state-of-the-art schemes..." is correct.

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: The phrase "We show the performance results of CARE over state-of-the-art schemes" contains no evaluative adjective — "substantially," "significantly," "dramatically" are all absent. The judgment is withheld for the data.

**APPLY**: Read your opening sentence and delete every adjective that predicts the outcome; if the sentence loses its meaning, rewrite it as a pure scope statement.

---

## 2. The Mandatory First Sentence

### The Problem This Rule Solves

The most common student failure in evaluation writing is opening a results subsection with a claim or a number: "Zion achieves 43.2% speedup, which is the best among all evaluated prefetchers." This construction puts the conclusion before the evidence and gives the reader no figure to anchor against. Both Zion and CARE open every results subsection the same way — with a sentence that names the figure, states the metric, and states the breadth of evaluation. The claim follows in sentence two.

---

**RULE 2.1**: Every results subsection begins with a figure-pointer sentence in one of three forms: "Figure N shows [metric] [scope]," "Figure N presents [metric] [scope]," or "Figure N illustrates [metric] [scope]." The sentence names the figure, the metric being displayed, and the breadth of the evaluation. It never begins with a number and never begins with a claim.

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "Figure 5(a) presents the normalized speedup of all evaluated prefetchers running 37 homogeneous SPEC workloads under various system configurations, including 1-core (Apple A14 and GLC), as well as multi-core (2/4/8-core GLC)."

Decomposed: Figure name (Figure 5(a)) + metric (normalized speedup) + scope (all evaluated prefetchers, 37 homogeneous SPEC workloads) + breadth (various system configurations, named explicitly).

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "For multi-copy SPEC workloads, Figure 7 shows the normalized IPC of schemes followed by the geometric mean across all the SPEC workloads."

Decomposed: Scope qualifier (for multi-copy SPEC workloads) + Figure name (Figure 7) + metric (normalized IPC of schemes) + breadth qualifier (geometric mean across all SPEC workloads).

**APPLY**: Write the figure-pointer sentence first, before the claim — the figure pointer is the door the reader walks through; the claim is what they find on the other side.

---

**RULE 2.2**: The figure-pointer sentence must name the metric precisely as it appears in the figure's y-axis label. "Speedup" is not the same as "normalized speedup." The sentence encodes the unit and normalization basis.

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "normalized speedup" — this matches Figure 5's y-axis label "Normalized Speedup." The word "normalized" is not omitted.

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "normalized IPC" — note that CARE measures IPC (instructions per cycle) while Zion measures speedup; both name the metric precisely and neither says "performance" as a vague substitute.

**APPLY**: Never write "Figure N shows the performance of..." — "performance" is not a metric; name the actual metric (IPC, speedup, miss rate, latency, throughput) and its normalization basis.

---

**RULE 2.3**: When a single subsection discusses multiple figures (e.g., a primary result and a supporting measurement), the secondary figure is introduced with "Similarly, Figure N shows..." or "Figure N compares..." — maintaining the figure-pointer pattern for every figure in the subsection.

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "Figure 8 compares the LLC pure miss rate (pMR) with different schemes for the 30 memory-intensive SPEC workloads." (This appears after the main IPC result from Figure 7, in the same subsection.)

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "Figure 6 shows that Zion achieves LLC coverage rates of 73.6%, 84.0%, and 90.5% on SPEC 2006, SPEC 2017, and self-attention workloads, respectively." (Introduces a supporting metric in the coverage subsection.)

**APPLY**: Every figure reference requires its own figure-pointer sentence — never embed a second figure reference inside the middle of a paragraph without a sentence that names the figure, metric, and scope.

---

## 3. The 5-Move Results Paragraph Template

### Overview

Every results paragraph — regardless of what the result is — follows the same five-move structure. Moves 1 and 2 are mandatory. Moves 3, 4, and 5 are applied selectively based on what the data shows. The paragraph never ends on a raw number; it always ends on a causal or synthesis sentence.

### The Template

```
Move 1 — ORIENTATION:    "Figure N shows/presents [metric] across [scope]."
Move 2 — HEADLINE CLAIM: "[SYSTEM] achieves/outperforms [numbers], respectively."
                         OR "Across all [configs], [SYSTEM] consistently outperforms all [class]."
Move 3 — DRILL-DOWN:     "In particular, under [optimal config], [SYSTEM] surpasses
                         [A], [B], [C], and [D] by [x%], [y%], [z%], and [w%], respectively."
Move 4 — SECONDARY OBS:  "Moreover, [related trend or secondary metric observation]."
Move 5 — CAUSAL EXPL:    "[Trend] because [mechanism]."
                         OR "[SYSTEM] outperforms because [design reason]."
```

---

### Annotated Example from Zion (SPEC Performance Subsection)

**Move 1 — ORIENTATION:**
> "Figure 5(a) presents the normalized speedup of all evaluated prefetchers running 37 homogeneous SPEC workloads under various system configurations, including 1-core (Apple A14 and GLC), as well as multi-core (2/4/8-core GLC)."

This sentence does exactly one job: it tells the reader what Figure 5(a) shows. No claim is present.

**Move 2 — HEADLINE CLAIM:**
> "Across all configurations, Zion consistently outperforms all SOTA designs, achieves speedup of 25.5%, 38.5%, 40.9%, 43.2% and 32.5% over the baseline, respectively."

The word "respectively" maps numbers to configurations in the order they were named (1-core A14, 1-core GLC, 2-core, 4-core, 8-core). The word "consistently" is the headline judgment word — used once, at the broadest scope.

**Move 3 — DRILL-DOWN:**
> "In particular, under the optimal 4-core configuration, Zion surpasses IPCP, Bingo, SPP, and Berti by 18.2%, 10.1%, 8.0%, and 5.1%, respectively."

"In particular" signals the drill-down. The competitors appear in the same order they will appear in every subsequent comparison throughout the paper. The numbers map to competitors via "respectively."

**Move 4 — SECONDARY OBSERVATION (negative result):**
> "Moreover, the memory subsystem saturation, at a higher core count degrades the performance at 8-core for all prefetchers."

"Moreover" signals the secondary observation. This is a degradation case — but note that it is handled as an observation about ALL prefetchers, not a weakness specific to Zion. The scope of the degradation is bounded by "for all prefetchers."

**Move 5 — CAUSAL EXPLANATION (edge case):**
> "At the other end, on 1-core A14, the 6.4x smaller L2 cache capacity limits the gains of SPP, Berti, and Zion, resulting in a small performance gap."

The paragraph ends with a mechanism ("6.4x smaller L2 cache capacity limits the gains"), not a number. The reader leaves with an understanding, not a raw figure.

---

### Annotated Example from CARE (Multi-copy SPEC Subsection)

**Move 1 — ORIENTATION:**
> "For multi-copy SPEC workloads, Figure 7 shows the normalized IPC of schemes followed by the geometric mean across all the SPEC workloads."

**Move 2 — HEADLINE CLAIM (with normalization baseline stated):**
> "All results are normalized to the baseline of LRU. In the presence of prefetching, CARE achieves a geometric mean speedup of 10.3% over the LRU on the 30 memory-intensive benchmarks, while SHiP++, Hawkeye, Glider, and M-CARE improve performance over LRU by 7.6%, 6.2%, 7.2%, and 7.5%, respectively."

Note that CARE names ALL competitors in a single sentence with "respectively" — the reader can construct the ranking without rereading. The normalization baseline ("normalized to the baseline of LRU") is stated once before the first number appears.

**Move 3 — DRILL-DOWN (negative result handled inline):**
> "Figure 7 demonstrates that since CARE considers both locality and concurrency in cache management, CARE outperforms state-of-the-art schemes. LRU, SHiP++, Hawkeye, and Glider are the locality-based schemes. Although Hawkeye and Glider each perform well for some specific workloads, their performance improvements are not consistent across different benchmarks. Hawkeye yields performance below LRU baseline for 7 workloads. Glider performs worse than the LRU baseline for 5 workloads. This observation exhibits that the performance of the locality-based schemes is limited by access patterns."

The negative results for competitors are named explicitly (7 workloads, 5 workloads), given a class-level cause ("limited by access patterns"), and handled without hedging.

**Move 4 — SECONDARY OBSERVATION (supporting metric):**
> "Figure 8 compares the LLC pure miss rate (pMR) with different schemes for the 30 memory-intensive SPEC workloads."
> "On average, CARE and Glider yield the lowest average LLC pMR (0.50) than the other schemes. Table X shows the average PMC value of each LLC miss. In the CARE framework, the average PMC value is 95.11 cycles, which is less than 97.80 cycles by the second-best scheme (M-CARE in this case)."

The runner-up is named explicitly: "(M-CARE in this case)."

**Move 5 — SYNTHESIS (takeaway sentence):**
> "Compared to other schemes, CARE is not only more effective in reducing the number of LLC pure misses, but also in reducing the PMC values of LLC misses."

The paragraph ends on a synthesis sentence that names TWO dimensions of superiority, not a number. The reader understands why CARE wins, not just that it wins.

---

**RULE 3.1**: Move 1 (orientation) is mandatory for every results subsection and every figure reference. It is never optional.

**RULE 3.2**: Move 2 (headline claim) uses "consistently" at most once per subsection — for the broadest scope claim. Never write "consistently" for a claim about a single configuration.

**RULE 3.3**: Move 3 (drill-down) is introduced with "In particular," or "Notably," — never with "Interestingly," (which implies the author was surprised) or with no transition (which collapses Moves 2 and 3 into an undifferentiated list of numbers).

**RULE 3.4**: Move 5 (causal explanation) must name the mechanism, not restate the result. "Zion outperforms because of its ITSM design" is not a causal explanation. "The 6.4x smaller L2 cache capacity limits the gains of SPP, Berti, and Zion" names a mechanism.

**RULE 3.5**: The paragraph never ends on a number. If the last sentence contains a percentage, add a causal or synthesis sentence after it.

---

## 4. How to Report Numbers

### The Core Commitment

Number reporting in evaluation is not just about accuracy — it is a rhetorical commitment to a comparison order that must be maintained throughout the paper. Both Zion and CARE establish a competitor order in their first multi-system comparison and hold it for every subsequent comparison. Readers unconsciously track this order; violating it creates confusion and suggests the author cherry-picked the comparison sequence.

---

**RULE 4.1**: When reporting multiple configurations in the same sentence, list them in ascending order and report numbers in the same order, followed by "respectively."

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "Zion achieves speedup of 25.5%, 38.5%, 40.9%, 43.2% and 32.5% over the baseline, respectively." The configurations listed earlier in the sentence are 1-core A14, 1-core GLC, 2-core, 4-core, 8-core. The numbers 25.5%, 38.5%, 40.9%, 43.2%, 32.5% map to those configurations in that order.

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "CARE improves IPC by 10.3% over the LRU on the 30 memory-intensive benchmarks, while SHiP++, Hawkeye, Glider, and M-CARE improve performance over LRU by 7.6%, 6.2%, 7.2%, and 7.5%, respectively." Competitors are listed (SHiP++, Hawkeye, Glider, M-CARE) and numbers follow in the same order.

**APPLY**: Before finalizing any sentence with "respectively," verify that every number maps unambiguously to the item at the same position in the preceding list.

---

**RULE 4.2**: Multi-system comparison in one sentence. All competitors appear in a single sentence with "respectively." Never split one multi-system comparison across multiple sentences if all numbers are available.

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "In particular, under the optimal 4-core configuration, Zion surpasses IPCP, Bingo, SPP, and Berti by 18.2%, 10.1%, 8.0%, and 5.1%, respectively."

Four competitors, four numbers, one sentence. The reader does not need to re-read to find each competitor's deficit.

**EVIDENCE (CARE, GAP workloads)**: "In 16-core systems, CARE outperforms LRU, SHiP++, Hawkeye, Glider, Mockingjay, and M-CARE by 16.1%, 7.8%, 12.7%, 11.6%, 11.4%, and 7.3%, respectively."

Six competitors, six numbers, one sentence.

**APPLY**: If your sentence exceeds 60 words because of the competitor list, do not split it — instead, consider whether all competitors belong in the headline claim or whether some should be deferred to the drill-down.

---

**RULE 4.3**: Maintain consistent competitor order throughout the paper. Once you establish the order (e.g., IPCP, Bingo, SPP, Berti), use that order every time you list those competitors. Never reorder for narrative convenience.

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: The order IPCP, Bingo, SPP, Berti appears in Section I's contributions ("achieving performance improvements of 18.2%, 10.1%, 8.0% and 5.1% over these SOTAs"), is repeated in Section V-A's drill-down, and is maintained in Section V-B: "Zion outperforms IPCP, Bingo, SPP, and Berti by 11.8%, 10.0%, 4.4%, and 5.1%, respectively."

**APPLY**: Create a competitor-order table for your paper (Competitor | Abbreviation | Always-in-position) and check every comparison sentence against it before submission.

---

**RULE 4.4**: Name the runner-up explicitly, with a parenthetical identifier.

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "which is less than 97.80 cycles by the second-best scheme (M-CARE in this case)"

The phrase "(M-CARE in this case)" does two things: it tells the reader which scheme to watch, and it signals that the runner-up may vary by configuration — "in this case" hedges across configurations without weakening the claim about THIS configuration.

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: In the scalability section: "compared with a 11.9% improvement by the second-best scheme (Mockingjay in this case)"

**APPLY**: Whenever you write "the second-best scheme" or "the best baseline," name it parenthetically in the same sentence — never in the next sentence.

---

**RULE 4.5**: Use range reporting for worst-case or spread observations ("between X% and Y%"), not as a substitute for reporting individual numbers. Ranges are for bounding behavior, not for hiding variance.

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "the marginal improvement over the strongest competing prefetcher in any given workload is between 1.54% and 5.9%"

This sentence reports a spread (1.54%-5.9%) to characterize the distribution of per-workload advantages. It is used offensively — to establish that even the worst-case improvement is meaningful — not defensively to avoid reporting individual numbers.

**APPLY**: Use a range when you want to establish a floor ("at minimum X%") or describe a distribution; never use a range to replace individual numbers when the individual numbers are available and meaningful.

---

**RULE 4.6**: State the normalization baseline once, before the first number appears. After that, embed the baseline in the metric name (e.g., "speedup over LRU") rather than restating it sentence by sentence.

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "All results are normalized to the baseline of LRU." This sentence appears once, before the first number, and is never repeated. Subsequently the text reads "CARE achieves a geometric mean speedup of 10.3% over the LRU" — "over the LRU" is the embedded baseline reference.

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: The baseline is embedded in the metric name "speedup over the baseline" and in the figure label "Normalized Speedup." No standalone baseline-statement sentence is needed because the metric name carries the normalization.

**APPLY**: Choose either the CARE style (explicit one-time baseline sentence) or the Zion style (embedded in metric name), but never repeat the baseline statement more than once in the same subsection.

---

## 5. Transition Word Catalog

### Philosophy

Transition words in evaluation sections are not decorative. They are structural signals that tell the reader which move of the template they are in. Using the wrong transition word in the wrong position — or using a forbidden word that implies the author is interpreting results rather than reporting them — corrupts the reader's model of what they are reading.

---

### Required Transitions (use these; no substitutes)

| Transition | Move | Function | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| "Across all [X], [SYSTEM] consistently..." | Move 2 | Headline claim at broadest scope | "Across all configurations, Zion consistently outperforms all SOTA designs" |
| "In particular," | Move 3 | Drill-down to best or most notable config | "In particular, under the optimal 4-core configuration, Zion surpasses..." |
| "Similarly, Figure N shows..." | Move 1 (secondary figure) | Parallel experiment in same subsection | "Similarly, Figure 12 shows the performance improvement of cache management schemes across GAP workloads" |

---

### Optional Transitions (use at most once per subsection)

| Transition | Move | Function | Constraint |
|---|---|---|---|
| "Notably," | Move 3 | Most striking or counterintuitive result | AT MOST ONCE per subsection; never for an expected result |
| "Moreover," | Move 4 | Secondary observation or exception case | Do not use as a synonym for "In addition" — use only for observations that extend Move 2 |
| "As a result," | Move 5 | Causal explanation of trend | Follow immediately with the mechanism, not a restatement of the result |
| "due to [mechanism]" | Move 5 | Inline causal clause | Can appear mid-sentence; the mechanism must be specific, not generic |
| "because [reason]" | Move 5 | Inline causal clause | Same as above |
| "limiting [scope]" | Move 5 | Scope-bounded causal clause | Used to explain why degradation is bounded: "limiting the gains of SPP, Berti, and Zion" |

---

### Forbidden Transitions in Evaluation (never use these)

**RULE 5.1**: Never write "These results demonstrate that..." or any variant ("This shows that...", "This confirms...", "This validates...", "This proves..."). Results speak via data. The author's job is to report the data and name the mechanism, not to tell the reader what the data demonstrates.

**WHY**: "These results demonstrate that our system is effective" is circular — the reader already read the results. The sentence adds no information and signals that the author has confused meta-commentary for analysis.

**CORRECT REPLACEMENT**: Replace "These results demonstrate that CARE is more effective than locality-only schemes" with the actual mechanism: "CARE outperforms locality-only schemes because it reduces the PMC values of LLC misses in addition to reducing miss count."

---

**RULE 5.2**: Never write "However" for your own system's results. "However" signals a negative pivot. In evaluation, it belongs exclusively to the description of competitors' limitations in Background or Related Work, or to the handling of outlier cases that apply equally to all systems. Using "However" about your own system's performance in a comparison subsection implies your system has a weakness you are conceding — which should be handled via the negative-result template (Section 6), not via a casual adversative.

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "However" appears three times in the paper. Two uses are in Section II (Background) describing SPP and Berti's limitations: "However, its count-based confidence mechanism inherently underestimates entries." One use describes a universal degradation: "Moreover, the memory subsystem saturation, at a higher core count degrades the performance at 8-core for all prefetchers." None of the three uses describe Zion's own performance in a comparison.

**CORRECT REPLACEMENT**: If your system degrades in a specific configuration, use the negative-result template (Section 6) — name the condition, give the mechanism, and follow with a positive recovery.

---

**RULE 5.3**: Never use "Interestingly," in evaluation. This word implies the author was surprised by their own system's behavior, which signals inadequate design intuition to reviewers.

**RULE 5.4**: Never use "clearly" or "obviously" in evaluation. These words beg the question — if the result were obvious, the paper would not need to report it.

---

## 6. How to Handle Negative Results

### The Core Principle

Negative results — cases where your system underperforms, matches but does not exceed a baseline, or degrades from a prior configuration — are handled via a three-step pattern: (1) name the degradation explicitly, (2) give a mechanical cause in one clause, (3) bound or generalize the scope. Never end on degradation; always follow with a positive observation.

The first-year student instinct is to suppress negative results or report them vaguely ("performance varies across workloads"). Both Zion and CARE name degradation cases explicitly and with specific numbers. Specificity is what makes the treatment credible rather than defensive.

---

**RULE 6.1**: Name the degradation case with an explicit count or configuration.

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "the memory subsystem saturation, at a higher core count degrades the performance at 8-core for all prefetchers"

Specific configuration: 8-core. Scope: all prefetchers. No vagueness about which case degrades.

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "Hawkeye yields performance below LRU baseline for 7 workloads. Glider performs worse than the LRU baseline for 5 workloads."

Specific counts: 7 workloads, 5 workloads. These are competitor degradations, but the same pattern applies to your own system's degradations.

**APPLY**: Never write "performance may be lower in some configurations" — name the configuration and the count.

---

**RULE 6.2**: Give the mechanical cause in one clause immediately after naming the degradation. The cause must name a specific hardware or algorithmic mechanism, not a vague attribution ("due to the nature of the workload").

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "[degradation] at 8-core for all prefetchers" is followed immediately by a separate sentence naming the cause: "the memory subsystem saturation, at a higher core count degrades the performance" — the mechanism is memory subsystem saturation.

**EVIDENCE (Zion, edge case)**: "the 6.4x smaller L2 cache capacity limits the gains of SPP, Berti, and Zion" — mechanism: L2 cache capacity (quantified as 6.4x smaller). Scope: SPP, Berti, and Zion (not all prefetchers).

**APPLY**: The causal clause must be specific enough that a reader could design an experiment to test it — "because of workload characteristics" fails this test; "because the 6.4x smaller L2 cache capacity limits lookahead prediction depth" passes it.

---

**RULE 6.3**: Bound the scope of the degradation. Establish whether it is universal (all configurations), class-wide (all prefetchers in this category), or workload-specific (N out of M workloads).

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: "degrades the performance at 8-core for all prefetchers" — scope: universal at 8-core. This framing protects Zion: if ALL prefetchers degrade, Zion's degradation is not a design flaw.

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "This observation exhibits that the performance of the locality-based schemes is limited by access patterns" — scope: a class (locality-based schemes). CARE, being concurrency-aware, is positioned outside this class.

**APPLY**: Choose the scope carefully — "for all systems" defends your system by universalizing the degradation; "for N workloads" bounds the damage quantitatively; "for [specific competitor class]" positions your system as exempt.

---

**RULE 6.4**: Never end a paragraph on a degradation observation. Immediately follow the degradation with a positive observation about your system or a broader positive synthesis.

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: After "the memory subsystem saturation, at a higher core count degrades the performance at 8-core for all prefetchers," the next sentence is a positive edge-case explanation: "At the other end, on 1-core A14, the 6.4x smaller L2 cache capacity limits the gains of SPP, Berti, and Zion, resulting in a small performance gap." The paragraph pivots to mechanistic explanation of edge cases rather than dwelling on degradation.

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: After "Hawkeye yields performance below LRU baseline for 7 workloads. Glider performs worse than the LRU baseline for 5 workloads," CARE immediately follows with the positive explanation: "This observation exhibits that the performance of the locality-based schemes is limited by access patterns. M-CARE considers the miss concurrency but ignores the hit-miss overlapping..." This moves the reader from competitor degradations to an explanation of CARE's advantage.

**APPLY**: Draft the degradation paragraph, then add a required "recovery sentence" that either names the positive bound, names the mechanism, or pivots to a broader positive observation before the paragraph closes.

---

**RULE 6.5** (CARE pattern): When multiple competitors degrade, list them in parallel sentences before delivering the class-level explanation.

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "Hawkeye yields performance below LRU baseline for 7 workloads. Glider performs worse than the LRU baseline for 5 workloads. This observation exhibits that the performance of the locality-based schemes is limited by access patterns."

Two parallel sentences establish individual facts; one synthesis sentence delivers the class-level implication. This is a clean rhetorical structure — the synthesis sentence is clearly labeled as synthesis ("This observation exhibits that...") and names the class mechanism, not the individual systems.

Note: "This observation exhibits that..." is acceptable as a synthesis sentence in this specific pattern because it names a class-level mechanism rather than merely restating that the data is good. It is not the same as the forbidden "These results demonstrate that our system is effective."

**APPLY**: Use this parallel-sentence + synthesis pattern when you have two or more competitor degradations that share a common class-level cause.

---

## 7. Takeaway and Synthesis Patterns

### Implicit vs. Explicit Takeaways

Both Zion and CARE handle takeaways differently, reflecting two legitimate conventions. Zion uses the implicit takeaway: the causal explanation sentence (Move 5) IS the takeaway — there is no separate "Takeaway" paragraph. CARE uses the explicit synthesis sentence at the end of each result block. Both conventions are correct. The error is having neither — closing a results paragraph with a raw number and no synthesis.

---

**RULE 7.1** (Zion pattern — Implicit Takeaway): Close the results paragraph with a causal explanation that embeds the implication. No separate takeaway sentence is needed if the causal explanation is strong.

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: The SPEC performance subsection closes: "At the other end, on 1-core A14, the 6.4x smaller L2 cache capacity limits the gains of SPP, Berti, and Zion, resulting in a small performance gap." This sentence tells the reader why the gap narrows on A14 and implicitly explains what design property would need to change to close the gap — it is a takeaway disguised as a mechanism sentence.

**APPLY**: Test whether your closing sentence tells the reader something they could not have inferred from the numbers alone. If yes, it functions as an implicit takeaway and no additional sentence is needed.

---

**RULE 7.2** (CARE pattern — Explicit Synthesis): Close the result block with an explicit synthesis sentence that names the multi-dimensional advantage.

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "Compared to other schemes, CARE is not only more effective in reducing the number of LLC pure misses, but also in reducing the PMC values of LLC misses."

This sentence names TWO dimensions of superiority (pure miss count AND PMC values), which is precisely what sets CARE apart from M-CARE (which considers concurrency but not hit-miss overlapping). The sentence cannot be reduced to a number — it states a relational claim about the mechanism.

**APPLY**: If your system wins on multiple orthogonal metrics, use the CARE synthesis sentence pattern ("not only ... but also...") to make the multi-dimensional advantage explicit.

---

**RULE 7.3** (CARE scalability pattern — Front-loaded observations): For scalability or multi-condition experiments where three or more distinct observations must be reported, front-load them as a numbered list before presenting the data, then confirm each observation in order.

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: "We make three major observations from Figure 11. First, the opportunity for cache management increases with multiple cores due to increased pressure on the LLC. Second, CARE outperforms every state-of-the-art cache management scheme in all configurations. Third, as the number of cores increases, the performance advantage of CARE becomes greater and greater."

Three observations are stated before any numbers appear. The reader knows what to look for in the figure. The subsequent paragraphs then confirm each observation in order.

**APPLY**: Use this pattern when a single figure supports multiple independent claims — the front-loaded list prevents the paragraph from becoming a flat enumeration of numbers with no structure.

---

**RULE 7.4**: Never begin a takeaway sentence with "These results show/demonstrate/confirm/prove that..." Instead, state the implication directly.

**WRONG**: "These results demonstrate that Zion is an effective prefetcher for memory-intensive workloads."

**CORRECT** (Zion style): "As the core count increases, pattern interference increases and prefetcher adaptability becomes increasingly important." (This is the implicit takeaway for the self-attention scalability result — it names what the reader should infer from the pattern, framed as a causal claim rather than meta-commentary.)

**CORRECT** (CARE style): "Compared to other schemes, CARE is not only more effective in reducing the number of LLC pure misses, but also in reducing the PMC values of LLC misses."

## 8. Conclusion Blueprint

### Structure

Both papers write the conclusion as a single paragraph of five to seven sentences. Neither paper uses subsections ("In summary," / "Future work" / "Limitations") in the conclusion. Neither paper mentions specific percentages — numbers belong in the abstract and evaluation, not the conclusion. Neither paper uses the conclusion to summarize what was just read — the conclusion is a forward-looking characterization of the system's contribution and potential.

---

**RULE 8.1**: The conclusion is one paragraph, five to seven sentences. No subsections.

**EVIDENCE (Zion)**: The conclusion (Section VI) is a single continuous paragraph with no "Future work" paragraph attached.

**EVIDENCE (CARE)**: The conclusion (Section VIII) is a single continuous paragraph. The label "VIII. CONCLUSIONS" (plural) does not imply multiple subsections — the plural is a convention, the structure is a single paragraph.

---

**RULE 8.2**: The six-sentence structure.

| Sentence | Function | Pattern |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | System introduction | "This paper presents [SYSTEM], a [adjective] [type]." OR "In this paper, we emphasize [central insight]." |
| 2 | Primary mechanism to property | "By [mechanism], [SYSTEM] achieves [property 1]." OR "We propose [METRIC], [description]." |
| 3 | Secondary mechanism | "[SYSTEM] also [property 2] based on [mechanism 2]." |
| 4 (optional) | Additional property | "[SYSTEM] [additional property] for [scope]." |
| 5 | Evaluation result | "Evaluations show that [SYSTEM] consistently outperforms [class] across [breadth]." |
| 6 | Broader potential | "Overall, [SYSTEM] provides a practical foundation for [broader domain]." OR "[SYSTEM] has a true potential for [domain]." |

---

### Annotated Zion Conclusion

> "This paper presents a Zion prefetcher, a comprehensive and adaptive hardware prefetcher."

Sentence 1: system name, adjectives (comprehensive, adaptive), type (hardware prefetcher). The adjectives match the paper's three-word claim from the title and abstract — no new characterization is introduced in the conclusion.

> "By integrating ITSM with five specialized prediction modules, Zion achieves comprehensive coverage across diverse memory access patterns."

Sentence 2: mechanism (ITSM with five modules) + property (comprehensive coverage across diverse patterns). The mechanism is specific — "five specialized prediction modules" is not vague.

> "Additionally, Zion dynamically tunes prefetching behavior based on runtime feedback to mitigate memory pressure and enhance performance, while remaining lightweight."

Sentence 3: secondary mechanism (runtime feedback) + secondary property (mitigate memory pressure, lightweight). "Additionally" is used correctly here — the conclusion permits mild transitions that would be forbidden in evaluation.

> "Evaluations show that Zion consistently outperforms state-of-the-art prefetchers across diverse SPEC CPU and self-attention workloads."

Sentence 5 (evaluation result): no numbers, no specific percentages — the evaluation already stated those. "Consistently" is the headline claim word. "Diverse SPEC CPU and self-attention workloads" names the breadth.

The Zion conclusion omits Sentence 6 (broader potential statement) — this is a valid choice when the paper's scope is precisely bounded and the authors prefer not to overclaim future applicability.

---

### Annotated CARE Conclusion

> "In this paper, we emphasize the importance of data concurrency to memory performance."

Sentence 1: uses the "we emphasize [central insight]" form rather than "This paper presents [SYSTEM]." This form is appropriate when the paper's primary contribution is a new insight or metric rather than a new system.

> "We propose Pure Miss Contribution (PMC), a comprehensive metric used to weigh the performance cost of each cache miss."

Sentence 2: names the metric (PMC), gives its characterization (comprehensive), states its function (weigh the performance cost of each cache miss). No number is used.

> "We first develop a detailed measurement mechanism for PMC."

Sentence 3: secondary contribution (measurement mechanism). Brief — one clause.

> "Then, we utilize PMC to build CARE, a locality and concurrency-aware, lightweight cache management framework."

Sentence 4 (optional): names the system (CARE), gives its full characterization (locality and concurrency-aware, lightweight). The system name appears for the first time in Sentence 4, not Sentence 1 — because the PMC metric is the primary contribution.

> "CARE considers locality, concurrency, and overlapping to guide cache replacement decisions."

Sentence 5 (mechanism detail): adds the third dimension (overlapping) that distinguishes CARE from M-CARE. This sentence functions as an expanded Sentence 3 in the six-sentence template.

> "CARE is fully tested and analyzed. It outperforms state-of-the-art cache management schemes."

Sentence 6 (evaluation summary): two short sentences serving the evaluation-result function. No percentages.

> "Experimental and analysis results show CARE has a true potential for data-intensive scalable computing systems."

Sentence 7 (broader potential): "true potential for data-intensive scalable computing systems" is the broader-domain claim. Note that CARE says "has a true potential" — not "demonstrates potential" or "shows promise" — these are hedges. "Has a true potential" is a claim.

---

**RULE 8.3**: Never mention specific percentages in the conclusion. Percentages belong in the abstract and evaluation.

**EVIDENCE**: Neither Zion nor CARE quotes a specific percentage in its conclusion paragraph. Zion's conclusion contains no numbers at all. CARE's conclusion contains no numbers at all.

**APPLY**: If you find yourself writing "achieving 43.2% speedup" in your conclusion, move that number to the abstract and replace the conclusion sentence with the mechanism or property that produced that speedup.

---

**RULE 8.4**: Never write limitations in the conclusion. Handle limitations inline in the evaluation as exception cases with causal explanations (Section 6). A separate limitations paragraph signals that the authors were unable to handle the limitation within the evaluation's scope — reviewers will notice.

**EVIDENCE**: Neither Zion nor CARE contains a limitations paragraph anywhere in the paper. The 8-core degradation case in Zion is handled inline in Section V-A with a causal explanation ("memory subsystem saturation"), not deferred to the conclusion.

---

**RULE 8.5**: Never write "future work" in the conclusion. If a direction is obvious and high-value, a reviewer will suggest it regardless. If it is not obvious, you are directing reviewers toward unfinished work.

**EVIDENCE**: Neither Zion nor CARE mentions future work in its conclusion.

---

**RULE 8.6**: Never begin the conclusion with "In this paper, we showed that..." This construction announces a summary of what was just read. The conclusion characterizes the contribution and its potential, not what the paper showed.

**WRONG**: "In this paper, we showed that Zion achieves 43.2% speedup."

**CORRECT** (Zion form): "This paper presents a Zion prefetcher, a comprehensive and adaptive hardware prefetcher."

**CORRECT** (CARE form): "In this paper, we emphasize the importance of data concurrency to memory performance."

The distinction: "we showed" is backward-looking (what we did); "we emphasize" is a forward-casting claim about what the reader should carry away.

## 9. Quick-Reference Sentence Starters

### Every Evaluation Move, Ready to Fill In

Copy the pattern that matches your move. The brackets are mandatory fill-ins; do not leave them as placeholders.

---

**Move 1 — ORIENTATION** (mandatory, every subsection)

```
Figure [N] presents the [metric] of [what entities] [across/on/under] [scope].
Figure [N] shows the [metric] across [configurations] for [workload set].
For [workload class], Figure [N] shows the [metric] [followed by / including] [aggregation method].
```

---

**Move 2 — HEADLINE CLAIM** (mandatory, every subsection)

```
Across all [configurations/workloads/settings], [SYSTEM] consistently outperforms all [class of competitors].
[SYSTEM] achieves [X%], [Y%], [Z%] [on/over] [A], [B], [C], respectively.
In the presence of [setting], [SYSTEM] achieves a [geometric mean / average] [metric] of [X%] over [baseline],
  while [Comp1], [Comp2], and [Comp3] [achieve/improve] by [a%], [b%], and [c%], respectively.
```

---

**Move 3 — DRILL-DOWN** (use when best result is at a specific configuration)

```
In particular, under [optimal configuration], [SYSTEM] surpasses [A], [B], [C], and [D]
  by [x%], [y%], [z%], and [w%], respectively.
Notably, in [configuration], [SYSTEM] outperforms [best competitor] by [margin],
  the strongest result across all evaluated [conditions].
```

---

**Move 4 — SECONDARY OBSERVATION** (use for supporting metric or exception)

```
Moreover, [secondary metric or trend observation].
[Figure N] compares [secondary metric] with different schemes for [scope].
On average, [SYSTEM] and [Comp] yield the [lowest/highest] [metric] ([value]) than the other schemes.
```

---

**Move 5 — CAUSAL EXPLANATION** (mandatory paragraph-closer)

```
[Trend] because [specific mechanism].
The [component] [verb] the [metric] by [action], limiting [scope].
As a result, [SYSTEM] in [configuration] outperforms [competitors] because [mechanism].
[SYSTEM] outperforms because [design reason]; [Comp] is limited because [class-level mechanism].
```

---

**Negative Result Pattern** (for degradation cases)

```
[SYSTEM / Competitor] yields [metric below / worse than] [baseline] for [N] workloads.
[Competitor2] performs worse than [baseline] for [M] workloads.
This observation exhibits that [class-level mechanism].
```

---

**Scalability Front-load Pattern** (for multi-observation figures)

```
We make [N] major observations from Figure [N].
First, [observation 1].
Second, [observation 2].
Third, [observation 3].
```

---

**Synthesis / Takeaway Pattern** (end of result block)

```
Compared to other schemes, [SYSTEM] is not only more effective in [metric 1], but also in [metric 2].
As [condition] increases, [SYSTEM's advantage] grows because [mechanism].
```

---

**Runner-up Naming Pattern**

```
..., which is [better/worse] than the [second-best/best baseline] scheme ([Competitor] in this case).
```

---

**Normalization Baseline Statement** (state once, before first number)

```
All results are normalized to the baseline of [system].
```

---

**Conclusion Sentence Templates**

```
Sentence 1: "This paper presents [SYSTEM], a [adj1] and [adj2] [type]."
            "In this paper, we emphasize [central insight]."

Sentence 2: "By [mechanism], [SYSTEM] achieves [property 1]."
            "We propose [METRIC], [description of what it measures]."

Sentence 3: "[SYSTEM] also [property 2] based on [mechanism 2]."
            "We [develop/implement] [component], a [description]."

Sentence 4: "Then, we [use mechanism to build] [SYSTEM], a [full description including key adjectives]."
  (optional — use when system and primary contribution are separate entities)

Sentence 5: "Evaluations show that [SYSTEM] consistently outperforms [class] across [breadth]."
            "[SYSTEM] is fully tested and analyzed. It outperforms [class]."

Sentence 6: "Overall, [SYSTEM] provides a practical [and extensible] foundation for [broader domain]."
            "[SYSTEM] has a true potential for [domain]."
```

## 10. Common Failure Modes

### The Seven Things PhD Students Consistently Get Wrong

These failure modes are listed in order of damage to the paper's acceptance probability. Failure Mode 1 is the most common and the most damaging; Failure Mode 7 is common but recoverable.

---

**FAILURE MODE 1: The Results Dump — Numbers Without Structure**

*What it looks like:* "Zion achieves 25.5%, 38.5%, 40.9%, 43.2%, and 32.5% on the five configurations. IPCP achieves 16.7%, 31.2%, 35.8%, 37.4%, and 28.1%, respectively. Bingo achieves..."

*Why it fails:* The reader cannot determine which number is the headline claim, which is the drill-down, and which is the secondary observation. The paragraph has no hierarchy. The reviewer must do the analytical work the author should have done.

*The fix:* Apply the 5-Move template. Start with Move 1 (the figure pointer). Deliver Move 2 as the first sentence about your system. Deliver Move 3 for the best configuration only. End with Move 5 (causal explanation).

*Evidence:* Zion's SPEC subsection (16 words of orientation + 1 headline claim sentence + 1 drill-down sentence + 1 secondary observation + 1 causal explanation) covers the same data as a results dump would spread over 10 sentences — but with hierarchy and a take-home.

---

**FAILURE MODE 2: The Missing Figure-Pointer Sentence**

*What it looks like:* "CARE achieves 10.3% speedup over LRU, while SHiP++ achieves 7.6%, Hawkeye achieves 6.2%, and Glider achieves 7.2%."

*Why it fails:* The reader has no figure to anchor against. If they are reading on paper, they must search for the figure; if they are at a talk, there is no visual anchor. The figure-pointer sentence is an accessibility requirement, not a stylistic preference.

*The fix:* Insert "Figure N shows [metric] for [scope]." before every number-reporting sentence that is supported by a figure.

*Evidence:* Both Zion and CARE open EVERY results subsection and EVERY secondary metric with a figure-pointer sentence. There is no exception in either paper.

---

**FAILURE MODE 3: No Causal Explanation — Paragraphs End on Numbers**

*What it looks like:* "...In particular, under the 4-core configuration, Zion surpasses IPCP, Bingo, SPP, and Berti by 18.2%, 10.1%, 8.0%, and 5.1%, respectively."

*Why it fails:* The paragraph ends on numbers. The reader knows the measurement but not the mechanism. The reviewer asks: "Why does Zion win by more on IPCP than on Berti? What does that tell us about the design?" If the author does not answer this in the paragraph, the reviewer will answer it themselves — and may answer incorrectly.

*The fix:* Add a causal sentence after the last number: "Zion's advantage over IPCP is largest because IPCP's unified IP table accumulates conflicts in multi-core configurations, while ITSM's segmented tables prevent this."

*Evidence:* Both Zion and CARE end every results paragraph with a mechanism sentence, never with a percentage.

---

**FAILURE MODE 4: The Vague Scoping Paragraph**

*What it looks like:* "In this section, we evaluate the performance of our system. We compare against several state-of-the-art baselines using multiple metrics across various workloads."

*Why it fails:* This paragraph contains no information. "Several baselines" is not named. "Multiple metrics" is not specified. "Various workloads" is not bounded. It tells the reader nothing they will not learn from the first subsection heading.

*The fix:* Either delete the paragraph (Zion approach) or replace it with a single specific sentence (CARE approach): "[SYSTEM] is evaluated against [named competitors] using [specific metric] in [specific configuration] with [specific setting]."

*Evidence:* CARE's scoping sentence is exactly 22 words and names all four required elements (system, comparison class, configuration, setting). Zion omits it entirely. Neither paper writes a vague three-sentence preamble.

---

**FAILURE MODE 5: Hedged Conclusions**

*What it looks like:* "Overall, CARE may provide improved performance in many cache management scenarios, with potential to generalize to a broader range of systems."

*Why it fails:* "May provide," "many scenarios," and "potential to generalize" are all hedges. The conclusion is the one place in the paper where the author is permitted — and expected — to make a strong, unqualified claim about the system's contribution. A hedged conclusion signals that the authors are not confident in their own work.

*The fix:* Replace hedges with direct claims. "May provide improved performance" becomes "provides a practical foundation for." "Potential to generalize" becomes "has a true potential for."

*Evidence:* CARE: "CARE has a true potential for data-intensive scalable computing systems." This is not hedged. Zion: "Zion consistently outperforms state-of-the-art prefetchers across diverse SPEC CPU and self-attention workloads." This is not hedged.

---

**FAILURE MODE 6: Competitor Order Inconsistency**

*What it looks like:* Section V-A lists "IPCP, Bingo, SPP, Berti." Section V-B lists "Berti, IPCP, SPP, Bingo." Section V-C lists "Bingo, SPP, IPCP, Berti."

*Why it fails:* The reviewer's eye tracks competitors as a unit. If the order changes, the reviewer must re-read each sentence to match names to numbers. This creates cognitive overhead that makes the evaluation feel sloppy.

*The fix:* Establish competitor order on first appearance and enforce it mechanically throughout. Create a table: [Competitor | Position | Appears in figures as]. Check every comparison sentence before submission.

*Evidence:* Zion uses IPCP, Bingo, SPP, Berti in Sections I, V-A, and V-B consistently. The order is not alphabetical and not by performance — it is by design similarity to Zion's approach, introduced in the background section.

---

**FAILURE MODE 7: The Summary Conclusion**

*What it looks like:* "In this paper, we proposed Zion, a hardware prefetcher. We showed that Zion outperforms IPCP by 18.2%, Bingo by 10.1%, SPP by 8.0%, and Berti by 5.1% in the 4-core configuration. These results demonstrate that Zion is effective for memory-intensive workloads."

*Why it fails:* The conclusion repeats numbers already in the paper (redundant), uses the forbidden "These results demonstrate that" (meta-commentary), and characterizes the system as merely "effective" (vague). The reader learns nothing from the conclusion that they did not already know.

*The fix:* Write the conclusion as a forward-casting characterization, not a backward-looking summary. Name the mechanism, not the numbers. Name the broader potential, not the specific result.

*Evidence:* Zion's conclusion does not repeat the 18.2%, 10.1%, 8.0%, 5.1% numbers from Section V-A. CARE's conclusion does not repeat the 10.3% IPC improvement from Section VII. Both conclusions characterize the system's properties and potential — they do not summarize the paper's measurements.

---

### Summary: The Non-Negotiable Rules

If every rule in this chapter were condensed to a checklist for the night before submission, these ten items are the ones that, if violated, will cause a rejection or a major revision:

1. Every results subsection begins with a figure-pointer sentence naming the figure, metric, and scope. No exceptions.
2. The headline claim follows Move 1. It never precedes it.
3. Every paragraph ends with a causal explanation or synthesis sentence, never with a number.
4. Competitor order is established on first appearance and maintained throughout the paper.
5. "These results demonstrate that..." and all its variants are deleted. Replaced with mechanism sentences.
6. "However" is never used about your own system's results in a comparison context.
7. Negative results are named with a specific count or configuration, given a mechanical cause, and bounded in scope.
8. The conclusion contains no percentages, no "future work," no "limitations," and no "we showed that."
9. The normalization baseline is stated once, before the first number, and embedded in the metric name thereafter.
10. The runner-up is named explicitly with a parenthetical identifier in the same sentence where it is invoked.

- `These results confirm that...` — meta-commentary; replace with mechanism sentence
- `These results demonstrate that...` — circular meta-commentary; replace with mechanism sentence
- `These results prove that...` — meta-commentary; replace with mechanism sentence
- `These results show that...` — meta-commentary; replace with mechanism sentence
- `These results validate that...` — meta-commentary; replace with mechanism sentence
- `tries to` — hedge; replace with declarative verb
- `Unfortunately,` — no place in a technical paper
- `utilize` — academic filler; use "use"
- `various workloads` (without quantification) — not reproducible; name the metric, threshold, and baseline
- `very` (as intensifier in abstract or introduction) — vacuous; delete; if you need emphasis, provide a number
- `we believe` — hedges and uses first-person uncertainty; replace with "We show" or "We find"
- `We make the following contributions:` — non-standard; use exact canonical forms listed above
- `We showed that...` (in conclusion) — backward-looking; use "This paper presents..." or "In this paper, we emphasize..."
